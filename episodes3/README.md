Part 3 begins after Part 2 reframed the Trinity Model — sea, air, and soil — not as a single perfect climate solution, but as **auxiliary wheels** that can partially support lost natural functions.

This part asks how far those auxiliary wheels can be attached to society:

```text
Which seas can be approached first?
Which cities can integrate cooling and airflow as infrastructure?
Which watersheds can connect soil restoration, flood control, and ocean impacts?
What can be implemented now, and what must be left to future generations?
```

## Part 3 Concept

Part 1 began with the search for a single climate solution.

Part 2 let go of that framing and reinterpreted the Trinity Model as auxiliary wheels:

```text
Let the sea breathe.
Cool the air and surface.
Return life to the soil.
```

Part 3 now shifts from concept and local practice toward social implementation:

- ocean-side auxiliary wheels: where deep-sea aeration could realistically begin
- air and urban auxiliary wheels: where mist cooling and airflow corridors can become infrastructure
- soil and watershed auxiliary wheels: where soil restoration, flood control, and ocean load reduction can connect
- governance, economics, culture, and regional differences
- the boundary between what can be done now and what must be passed to future generations
