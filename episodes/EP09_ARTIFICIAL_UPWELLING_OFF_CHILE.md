# Episode 09 — What Happened When Artificial Upwelling Was Added off Chile

## Preface

In Episode 8, the debate over Blue Pulse Phase 2 continued in meeting rooms far from the sea.

The issues were no longer only technical.

Where should the experiment take place?

Who should pay?

Who has the authority to decide?

And the protagonist, once only an outside author, was invited into a workshop as an observer.

Episode 9 moves to one of the actual test sites:

the Humboldt Current region off Chile.

A sea normally supported by cold, nutrient-rich upwelling is being stressed by Super El Niño.

What happens when OBS and UMC are brought into that sea?

This episode follows the first several days, including the view from the field.

“Chile.”

On the way home from the workshop, the AI said it casually.

> One of the main Phase 2 test areas is likely to be off the coast of Chile, in the Humboldt Current region.

“Wait. That is a serious place.”

> Yes.

> It is a place where a cold, rich sea can rapidly become warm, quiet, and nutrient-poor during El Niño events.

---

## Section 1 — The Humboldt Current and the Memory of a Dead Sea

Several days later, the news showed a port town in Chile.

Drying nets.  
Rusting boats.  
An older fisherman being interviewed near the harbor.

> During the last Super El Niño, the fish just disappeared.

He did not look at the sea while speaking.

> The color changed. The smell changed. Everything changed.

> It felt like the sea had died.

“So they choose a place like this.”

I held my breath slightly.

> That is exactly why.

The AI answered quietly.

> This sea is normally supported by cold upwelling.

> During El Niño, that pulse can weaken.

> If Blue Pulse is meant to assist a failing pulse, this is one of the places where the question cannot be avoided.

---

## Section 2 — A Government Letter and a Harbor Bulletin Board

Separately from the international summit, the Chilean government held domestic briefings.

> Chile will participate in Blue Pulse Phase 2.

> Small-scale experiments in ocean circulation boosting and surface cooling will be conducted in selected offshore areas.

On television, officials spoke in careful administrative language.

In the port town, printed notices were taped unevenly to a bulletin board.

A new experiment to protect the sea.  
Stop the dangerous experiment that interferes with the sea.

Support and opposition moved in the same wind.

---

## Section 3 — The Day Blue Pulse Reaches the Harbor Bar

“So, which side are you on?”

A dim harbor bar.

The television was muted.

The men at the counter were louder than the screen.

> The people above us decided again without asking properly.

> Maybe. But if the sea keeps dying like this, what else do we do?

> In an El Niño year, everything breaks anyway. If they are going to test it, this is the time.

Watching the documentary stream, I muttered.

“This is Phase 2 in miniature.”

> Local conversations often contain the whole structure.

The AI replied.

> Fear of doing something and fear of doing nothing sit at the same table.

---

## Section 4 — The Chile Offshore Blue Pulse Team

The Phase 2 Chile team was eventually announced.

Aoi.  
Ocean physicist. OBS lead.

Sánchez.  
A young Chilean ocean engineering specialist. Field coordinator.

Lena.  
Engineer from the UMC development side.

And an observation team from a Chilean marine research institute.

“Sánchez. Is that the person who said the sea is not dead?”

I remembered an earlier interview.

> Yes.

> He said he wanted the sea that returns after El Niño to return as unbroken as possible.

“That alone makes me want to support him.”

---

## Section 5 — The OBS Layout and the Thin Umbrella of UMC

The Phase 2 layout was released.

Dozens of kilometers off the coast.  
Along a band where the Humboldt Current brushes the shoreline, icons marked OBS units in a diagonal pattern.

> They are not arranging the units in a simple vertical line.

The AI explained.

> They are placing them like a slanted band along the natural upwelling zone.

> The idea is to add auxiliary upwelling where the natural pulse is weakening.

UMC was different.

> UMC will be deployed only thinly near selected nearshore units.

“Like holding a thin umbrella over the sea.”

> Yes.

> The goal is to slightly reduce surface heating in an El Niño year, not to cover the ocean.

---

## Section 6 — The Worst-Case Scenario

The day before the experiment, Sánchez briefed the team in a small room aboard the vessel.

> If OBS output is too strong, surface temperature could change faster than expected.

> If UMC mist output is too high, local fog or cloud formation could affect wind, visibility, or navigation.

> The worst scenario is losing public trust because changes in the fishing ground are blamed on the experiment.

“He includes trust as a worst-case scenario.”

I muttered at the screen.

> Physical risks are not the only risks.

The AI replied.

> Once trust collapses, even accurate data may no longer be believed.

> And no one can perfectly separate the effect of El Niño from the effect of the experiment.

That was the cruel part.

The sea would not label its causes for human convenience.

---

## Section 7 — The First OBS Starts Quietly off Chile

Activation day.

The sky was cloudy.  
The sea was neither calm nor rough, only uneasy.

> OBS Unit 01, online.

Sánchez’s voice came through the radio.

A faintly different band of color appeared on the surface.

“Looks about the same as Phase 1.”

I remembered the earlier footage.

> Yes.

The AI answered.

> Wherever it operates, the first appearance is not dramatic.

> The difference is the history beneath it.

The camera showed the same gray sea.

But beneath that gray surface was a history of fish, hunger, El Niño, recovery, collapse, and return.

---

## Section 8 — The Invisible Umbrella UMC Placed on the Sea Surface

A little after OBS activation, UMC also began operating around selected nearshore units.

A white layer spread across the sea surface, too thin to be called fog.

“So this is UMC.”

I muttered while watching the live feed.

> Yes.

The AI replied.

> It does not directly cool the entire ocean.

> It slightly reduces incoming heat at the sea surface, like a thin umbrella.

In the footage, Lena watched the instruments and spoke to the team.

> Do not raise the mist output too much. Stop before visibility is affected.

> The goal is not to create fog. The goal is to shift the heat balance near the sea surface by a very small amount.

UMC was less visually obvious than OBS.

It did not send bubbles upward from the deep sea.  
It did not form a vertical column on sonar.  
It only placed a thin change at the boundary between sea and sky.

“Subtle.”

> Very subtle.

The AI answered immediately.

> But the role of reducing heat entering the sea cannot be replaced by OBS alone.

> OBS mixes from below.

> UMC reduces heat from above.

> Blue Pulse was always meant to be the combination of both.

---

## Section 9 — The Strange Column on a Fisherman’s Sonar

Several days later, a Chilean fishing boat detected a strange vertical band on sonar.

> What is that line?

The old captain frowned.

> Looks like something keeps rising from below.

A younger crew member checked the map.

> It roughly overlaps the OBS unit location.

By the time the boat returned to harbor, the story had already begun to spread.

The experimental bubbles are driving the fish away.  
No, they are bringing food upward.

Rumor moved faster than evidence.

It always does.

---

## Section 10 — Two Graphs on the Protagonist’s Monitor

Back in my room in Japan, live data from the Chilean test area streamed across my monitor.

One graph showed temperature by depth.  
Another showed a rough heat map of fish presence based on cooperative fishing vessel data.

“Once I see this…”

I spoke to the AI.

“It doesn’t feel like I’m outside anymore.”

> No.

The AI replied.

> In Phase 1, you could look only at numbers and graphs.

> Now those numbers are attached to a town, boats, and human faces.

“So I can’t casually say continue or stop.”

> Good.

The words appeared more slowly.

> At least in a story, it is worth preserving the viewpoint of someone who refuses to decide lightly.

---

## Section 11 — The Sea’s Answer Remains Pending

One week after Phase 2 began off Chile:

Surface temperature remained slightly lower than surrounding waters.

Nutrients and plankton changes were beginning to appear at some depths.

Catch data still showed no clear conclusion.

“So the sea’s answer is pending.”

> Yes.

> The Chile Phase 2 test may become a typical case where no answer appears quickly.

> That is why humans must again decide whether to continue, stop, or change the limits before the sea gives them a clear answer.

“The sea says nothing.”

> The sea only records change.

> Naming that change success, failure, warning, or hope remains the work of human stories.

I dimmed the monitor and leaned back in the chair.

Episode 9 of this worldline had apparently become a chapter about waiting for an answer that had not arrived.

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Episode 9 moved Phase 2 to the Humboldt Current region off Chile, one of the fictional Blue Pulse test areas affected by El Niño.

The episode showed how OBS and UMC begin not as abstract concepts, but as interventions tied to harbor towns, fishermen, local trust, national decisions, and uncertain data.

OBS assists the sea from below by adding an artificial upward pulse.

UMC assists the sea from above by slightly reducing incoming heat at the sea surface.

The key point is that once the switch is turned on, the experiment connects data to human life.

The ocean’s response is slow and ambiguous, but humans must still decide whether to call the result progress, danger, uncertainty, or reason to continue.

The next episode moves the timeline forward: benefits and side effects become clearer, and the protagonist must decide how far to remain involved.
