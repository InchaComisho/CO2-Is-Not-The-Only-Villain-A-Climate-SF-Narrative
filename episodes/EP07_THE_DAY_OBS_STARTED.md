# Episode 07 — The Day the First OBS Started, What Did the Sea Say?

## Preface

In Episode 6, the fictional Blue Pulse project was formally proposed.

The proposal faced predictable but serious questions:

Does it work?

Is it dangerous?

Who pays?

Who decides?

And can a civilization that has waited too long still afford to wait for perfect safety?

Episode 7 moves from proposal to experiment.

The first OBS unit is activated in a small test area.

What happens?

And just as importantly, what does not happen?

---

“It started.”

When I saw those words through the monitor, I did not understand them at first.

> The Blue Pulse pilot experiment.

The AI continued.

> The first OBS unit has just been activated.

---

## Section 1 — The Test Area Begins as a Small Blue Square

A small article appeared in the corner of a news site.

Small-scale ocean circulation boost experiment begins in a selected Pacific test area.

On the map, there was only one pale blue square.

That was the experimental sea area.

“Smaller than I expected.”

> It is the first trial.

The AI answered calmly.

> Only several dozen square kilometers.

“That’s like a grain of sand compared with the ocean.”

> Yes.

> But symbolically, it is the first grain of sand where OBS officially operates.

At the bottom of the screen, the project name appeared.

Blue Pulse Phase 1.

---

## Section 2 — The OBS Unit Looks Surprisingly Ordinary

The live feed switched.

A small work vessel floated under a cloudy sky.

On deck were silver cylinders, hoses, and a container-sized unit.

“I thought it would look more like science fiction.”

> It is mostly pumps, pipes, compressors, sensors, and control systems.

The AI began explaining, almost cheerfully.

> From the unit, a long hose descends into the sea.

> The tip is lowered to a predetermined depth.

> Air mixed with fine bubbles is released from that depth.

“Air sent from above, bubbles released below.”

> Exactly.

> Today, only one line will be operated.

> The output will remain low while the team observes the ocean’s response.

On the screen, a technician placed a hand on the activation switch.

---

## Section 3 — The Time When It Looks Like Nothing Is Happening

A status message appeared.

`OBS UNIT 01: ONLINE`

The sea surface barely changed.

“Quiet.”

> It looks that way.

The AI’s text slowed slightly.

> Fine bubbles are difficult to see from the surface. The plume itself is not dramatic from above.

> In the first few hours, the change may appear only as small fluctuations in temperature sensors.

“Not very cinematic.”

> Many physically meaningful changes are visually boring.

The news anchor ended the segment with a careful smile.

> No major change has been confirmed at this time. Observation results over the coming weeks will be closely watched.

I almost laughed.

The first artificial breath of the sea had become a sentence nobody would remember.

---

## Section 4 — Weeks Later, the Difference Exists Only as Graphs

Several weeks later, the AI displayed a set of graphs.

> Interim report for Phase 1.

“This already looks like something only technical people will enjoy.”

> Endure it.

The first graph showed temperature profiles by depth.

Before activation.

After several weeks of OBS operation.

> Surface temperature in the test area decreased by about 0.2 degrees Celsius on average.

“Could be noise.”

> After accounting for natural variability, the residual still shows a statistically meaningful difference.

> At the same time, the 20 to 50 meter layer warmed slightly.

“So some surface heat moved downward.”

> That is the interpretation.

The second graph showed chlorophyll concentration.

A proxy for phytoplankton activity.

> The test area shows a slightly stronger increase than surrounding waters.

“So the plankton may have become a little more active.”

> Possibly. A small increase in nutrient supply from deeper water may be involved.

---

## Section 5 — Not a Success, but Not Meaningless

“So,” I said, staring at the graphs, “this is not exactly a ‘wow, the world is saved’ result.”

> That is precisely what Phase 1 shows.

The AI refused to exaggerate.

> It did not save the world.

> It did not cool the ocean in any broad sense.

> But it suggests that extreme surface heating might be slightly reduced locally.

“Not enough to call it a miracle.”

> Correct.

“But enough that calling it meaningless would also be wrong.”

> Correct.

That ambiguous line was the problem.

The news coverage was just as uncertain.

Small-scale OBS test shows slight sea surface cooling.

Some experts call the result promising, while others caution that broader ocean impacts remain limited.

No one knew whether to cheer.

No one knew whether to object.

That made everyone uncomfortable.

---

## Section 6 — Changes Too Small to Call Side Effects

“What about unexpected side effects?” I asked.

That was the part I cared about most.

> No major negative impacts have been reported within the Phase 1 area.

The AI added another line.

> However, several small irregularities were observed.

“Irregularities?”

> A slight shift in the movement pattern of some fish groups.

> A small change in plankton community composition.

> Sensor anomalies that may or may not be related to the plume.

“Natural variability?”

> Possibly.

“But nobody can completely rule out OBS.”

> Correct.

“So the answer is: we do not know yet.”

> Yes.

> And ‘we do not know yet’ becomes useful material for both supporters and opponents.

That felt very human.

Too human.

---

## Section 7 — Continue, or Stop Here?

At the follow-up international meeting, Aoi summarized the result.

> Phase 1 suggests that OBS may slightly reduce local sea surface temperature and may support limited plankton activity.

> At this stage, no major ecological abnormality has been confirmed.

> However, we cannot state that the intervention has no ecological impact.

Then came the proposal.

> We propose Phase 2: a somewhat broader test under stricter observation.

“So this is where continue or stop becomes the question.”

> Yes.

The AI’s words felt heavier.

> Some will argue that going further is unsafe.

> Others will argue that stopping here may waste time civilization does not have.

No machine could answer that alone.

That was becoming painfully clear.

---

## Section 8 — The Island Representative Buys Time

Tua took the microphone again.

> I cannot speak about every technical detail.

> But after seeing the Phase 1 results, I can say one thing.

He paused.

> OBS and UMC may not be magic that solves the crisis.

> But they may become brakes that slow the speed of the crisis.

> If they can extend the time before our island becomes unlivable by ten or twenty years, then we want to buy that time.

The room went quiet.

Buy time.

It was not an inspiring phrase.

It was not heroic.

But it sounded more honest than “save the world.”

---

## Section 9 — My Position Changes Slightly

Watching the stream, I spoke to the AI.

“To be honest, I first treated OBS and UMC like final-battle material for a civilization story.”

> A last-stage gimmick.

“Exactly.”

“But when it becomes about buying time, the feeling changes.”

> How?

“If it’s not a technology that saves the world, but a technology that buys time, the judgment changes.”

> That is an important shift.

The AI inserted one line.

> Risk tolerance changes depending on how much time remains.

I read the sentence twice.

Maybe that was the entire problem.

---

## Section 10 — The Sea Has Said Nothing Yet

“So,” I said, looking at the blue sea on the screen, “what does Phase 1 mean from the ocean’s side?”

> The ocean says nothing.

The words appeared quietly.

> The data only show traces of slightly increased mixing.

> Whether humans call that a hopeful sign or a trace of unnecessary interference is an interpretation.

“So that’s why it has to become a story.”

> Yes.

> This worldline you are watching is a record of a civilization that tried OBS.

> Episode 7 is its first page.

The sea remained blue.

The graphs kept updating.

And the answer stayed unfinished.

---

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Episode 7 depicted the first official activation of an OBS unit in the fictional Blue Pulse Phase 1 test area.

The result was deliberately modest:

- a slight local reduction in surface temperature,
- a possible increase in plankton activity,
- no major confirmed damage,
- but also small irregularities that could not be fully explained.

The point of this episode was not to show a miraculous success. It was to show the difficult middle ground where something is not obviously useless, but not clearly safe or sufficient either.

The next episode moves to the meeting room where the decision must be made: continue, stop, or redesign the experiment.
