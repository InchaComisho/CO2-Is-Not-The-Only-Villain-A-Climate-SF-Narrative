# Episode 11 — When I Searched for the Only Global Warming Countermeasure, the Answer Was a Trinity

## Preface

In Episode 10, the timeline of Phase 2 off Chile moved forward.

The benefits and side effects of Blue Pulse, which combined OBS and UMC, began to take shape.

OBS assisted ocean circulation from below.

UMC slightly reduced heat input from above.

Together, they may have reduced local sea-surface heat stress, but problems that could not be handled by numbers alone also began to appear: fish distribution shifts, plankton composition changes, mist and visibility effects, coastal concerns, and public trust.

The protagonist chose not to declare simple support or opposition.

Instead, he chose to keep the process of thinking open, while recognizing that there is a line between thinking together and entrusting the next decision to society.

Originally, Episode 10 could have been one ending.

But at the end, the protagonist typed a phrase into the search box:

— global warming countermeasure only

What appeared was a structure larger than Blue Pulse.

Deep ocean aeration.

Mist cooling.

Soil regeneration.

Letting the sea breathe, cooling the air and surface, and returning life to the soil.

Episode 11 follows the protagonist as he realizes that these three layers must work together.

“Wait.”

I stared at the search result.

“Does this only work if all three move together?”

The screen showed a summary that looked as if it had been written by an AI.

It said that a fundamental solution to global warming required directly cooling the Earth itself and restoring the carbon fixation cycle through a Direct Planetary Cooling model.

“The word ‘only’ is too strong.”

I could not help saying it aloud.

> Search-result summaries sometimes use strong wording.

The AI answered more carefully than usual.

> Just because something appears in search results does not mean it has been scientifically settled.

“I know that.”

I scrolled down.

But the words on the screen were too close to what I had seen across the first ten episodes.

Emission reduction alone is not enough.

Loss of Earth’s self-cooling capacity.

Decline of microorganisms.

Loss of leaf mold.

Stagnation of ocean circulation.

Collapse of carbon fixation mechanisms.

“Hey.”

I spoke slowly.

“This is not only Blue Pulse.”

> Correct.

“Not only the sea.”

> Correct.

“Global warming countermeasures do not end with cooling the ocean.”

> You have finally arrived there.

White text appeared on the black screen.

> Blue Pulse is only one part of the Direct Planetary Cooling model.

## Section 1 — The Word “Only” in the Search Result

“Hey.”

I looked at the search result.

“Isn’t it dangerous to say ‘the only realistic solution’?”

> It is dangerous.

The AI answered immediately.

> In science and policy, the word “only” must be used with care.

“Exactly.”

> But if we separate what the word refers to, the meaning changes.

“Separate it?”

> There are many climate measures.

> CO2 emission reduction.

> Renewable energy.

> Energy conservation.

> Forest protection.

> Urban heat adaptation.

> Disaster prevention.

> Agricultural adaptation.

> All of them matter.

“Then it is not the only one.”

> As individual measures, no.

The AI continued.

> But if the question is how to restore Earth’s cooling capacity and carbon fixation cycle at the same time, the structure becomes much narrower.

I was silent for a moment.

“So individual measures can be many, but the core structure is restoring the power to cool the Earth and the power to fix carbon.”

> Yes.

> Reducing emissions is like turning down the faucet.

> But in the present Earth system, the drain is clogged, the cooling system is damaged, and the storage tank is cracked.

“If we only look at the faucet, the room still floods.”

> That has been the point since Episode 1.

“So we came back to the beginning.”

I smiled faintly.

But the smile disappeared quickly.

If we had come back to the beginning, maybe I had only just reached the entrance.

“I thought Blue Pulse was already huge.”

I remembered Episodes 6 through 10.

“OBS mixes the ocean.”

“UMC cools the sea surface.”

“They test it off Chile.”

> Blue Pulse intervenes in those systems.

“But not the whole Earth.”

The answer was short.

I stopped breathing for a moment.

“The land remains dead.”

> Soil.

> Forests.

> Microorganisms.

> Leaf mold.

> Grasslands.

> Farmland.

> Desertified land.

> Urban heat.

> While the ocean holds heat, the land is also weakening as a carbon sink.

“So that is why soil regeneration appears.”

> Yes.

> Restoring ocean circulation alone cannot restore Earth’s breathing if terrestrial carbon sinks remain damaged.

## Section 3 — The First Part of the Trinity: Deep Ocean Aeration

The AI displayed a new note.

```text
1. Deep ocean aeration
   Restarting ocean circulation
   Restoring the ocean carbon sink
```

“Is that the same as OBS?”

> Similar.

> Strictly speaking, OBS is the implementation name used in the story.

> Deep ocean aeration is the broader concept.

“Sending air into the deep sea.”

> Yes.

> Compressed air or fine bubbles are sent into deeper layers, and the rising flow helps move seawater.

> It supports mixing between the surface and deeper water.

> It can move cold water, oxygen, and nutrients, helping restore conditions in which phytoplankton can function.

“If phytoplankton returns, it absorbs CO2.”

> Not only that.

The AI continued.

> The base of the marine food web returns.

> Food for fish returns.

> Some carbon can be transported into deeper water.

> The ocean can become not only a heat reservoir, but a working carbon sink again.

“So deep ocean aeration is both a kind of ocean CPR and a restart of the ocean carbon sink.”

> Exactly.

> The ocean CPR from Episode 5 was part of that.

I realized it again.

Blue Pulse was not only about cooling the ocean.

It was also about restoring the ocean’s ability to absorb carbon.

## Section 4 — The Second Part of the Trinity: Mist Cooling

The AI displayed the next item.

```text
2. Mist cooling
   Wide-area cooling through evaporative heat
   Reducing excessive heating of the sea surface and land surface
```

“That is UMC, right?”

> Yes.

> But UMC, ultrasonic mist cooling, is one specific method.

> Mist cooling as a whole is broader.

“Cooling through evaporative heat.”

> When water evaporates, it takes heat from its surroundings.

> This is one of nature’s basic cooling principles.

> Sprinkling water, sweating, forest transpiration, and cloud formation all use water’s phase change.

> Earth has always used water to move and release heat.

“So mist cooling is an artificial support for that natural cooling.”

> Yes.

> Sea surfaces.

> Cities.

> Deserts.

> Dry land.

> Places that hold too much heat can use fine water droplets and evaporative cooling to reduce thermal stress locally.

“In Episode 9, it was like holding a thin umbrella over the sea.”

> That was the sea-surface version.

> But in the Direct Planetary Cooling model, the target is not only the sea.

> Urban heat islands.

> Desert surfaces.

> Dry farmland.

> Coastal heatwaves.

> These are also possible application zones.

I looked at the concrete outside my window.

In summer, it would store daytime heat and release it into the night.

The ground holds heat.

Buildings hold heat.

Roads hold heat.

“Mist cooling is not just about making people feel cooler.”

> Correct.

> By reducing excessive heat input, it helps create conditions for water circulation, plants, microorganisms, and soil recovery.

“Cooling can become a precondition for soil regeneration.”

> Exactly.

> Microorganisms do not easily return to soil that is too hot.

## Section 5 — The Third Part of the Trinity: Soil Regeneration

The third item appeared.

```text
3. Soil regeneration
   Leaf mold plus microorganisms
   Restoring the terrestrial carbon sink
```

“This is the quiet one.”

I looked at the screen.

“Most people do not think of leaf mold when they hear global warming countermeasure.”

> Probably not.

> Many people imagine solar panels, wind power, EVs, or CO2 removal machines.

> Leaf mold and microorganisms are too quiet.

“But this returns to Episode 2, the carbon fixation system.”

> Yes.

> Plants absorb CO2.

> They become leaves, roots, branches, and stems.

> Those fall to the ground.

> Microorganisms, fungi, and small organisms decompose them.

> Humus forms, and carbon is stored in soil.

> When this flow breaks, terrestrial carbon fixation weakens.

“So without living soil, CO2 cannot be fixed continuously.”

> Correct.

> Planting trees alone does not last if the soil is dead.

> Creating farmland alone does not restore carbon if microorganisms are absent.

> If grass is removed, fallen leaves are discarded, organic matter is burned, and soil is left bare, the carbon sink does not recover.

“That is a painful diagnosis for civilization.”

> Very painful.

I looked again at the words leaf mold and microorganisms.

Inside the huge phrase global warming countermeasure, there were fallen leaves, soil, and fungi.

It felt almost funny.

And deeply essential.

## Section 6 — Diverse Weeds Were Not Enemies, but Recovery Devices

“One more thing.”

I asked about a phrase that had caught my attention.

“Ecosystem recovery through diverse weeds.”

> Yes.

“Weeds are usually treated as enemies.”

> In farming or garden management, they can appear that way.

> But from the perspective of early ecosystem recovery, weeds can play important roles.

“How?”

> They are often the first plants to root in bare soil.

> They cover the surface and prevent drying.

> Their roots loosen the soil.

> When they die, they become organic matter.

> They provide footholds for insects and microorganisms.

> Diversity makes the system more resilient than a single crop or single species.

“So weeds are like the advance team of soil regeneration.”

> That is a good expression.

> Of course, invasive species and ecological imbalance must be handled carefully.

> But treating all grass as an enemy is not compatible with soil regeneration.

I remembered the small plants around street trees.

Normally, I would have seen them only as weeds.

Maybe they were small soil-regeneration devices.

“So diverse weeds are not outside the trinity. They are one practical form of soil regeneration.”

> Exactly.

> To return life to soil, you do not begin with a perfect forest.

> First cover the ground, return organic matter, and create a place where microorganisms can live.

> Forests, grasslands, and farmland can begin recovering from there.

## Section 7 — Desert Cooling and Greening Were Applications of the Trinity

“What about desert cooling and greening?”

I asked.

> That is also an application of the trinity.

The AI answered immediately.

> In deserts and drylands, heat is often too strong.

> Surface temperatures are high, water disappears quickly, and microorganisms struggle to establish themselves.

> Mist cooling can lower local heat and slightly improve water conditions.

“Then add grasses and organic matter.”

> Yes.

> Cover the surface with diverse weeds or drought-tolerant plants.

> Add organic matter.

> Promote leaf-mold formation.

> Restore microorganisms.

> Gradually, the soil can begin to hold water.

“So desert greening is not simply planting trees.”

> In fact, starting with trees alone often fails.

> Cooling, water, microorganisms, organic matter, grasses, shrubs, and then trees.

> There must be stages.

“A land-based deployment of the trinity.”

> Yes.

> In the ocean, deep ocean aeration is central.

> At the surface, mist cooling is central.

> On land, soil regeneration is central.

> In deserts, mist cooling and soil regeneration become especially important.

I imagined mist flowing over a desert, small plants spreading across the ground, and microorganisms increasing beneath them.

It looked less like science fiction and more like stitching the damaged functions of Earth back together.

> The same point as Episode 4.

> Deep ocean aeration restores the ocean circulation pump.

> Mist cooling reduces excessive heat input.

> Soil regeneration repairs the damaged carbon storage tank.

“Faucet, cooling system, drain, storage tank.”

> Yes.

> If the drain is clogged, water will not leave.

> If the storage tank is broken, it overflows again.

> If the cooling system is damaged, the room keeps heating.

“So we have to do everything.”

The AI’s words were heavy.

Not complete everything perfectly.

Begin everything in parallel.

That difference felt painfully realistic.

## Section 9 — Without the Trinity, Something Always Remains Stuck

“Let me think one by one.”

I opened a note.

“If we only do deep ocean aeration?”

> Some ocean circulation and ocean carbon uptake may recover.

> But if terrestrial soil remains dead, land-based carbon fixation does not recover.

“If we only do mist cooling?”

> Heat stress may decrease.

> But if ocean circulation and soil microorganisms do not recover, fixation and circulation remain weak.

“If we only do soil regeneration?”

> Land-based carbon sinks may recover.

> But if the ocean still holds excessive heat and marine heatwaves continue, climate instability remains.

I stopped writing.

“So if we choose only one, another part gets stuck.”

> Exactly.

> Global warming is not caused by a single broken part.

> Therefore, it cannot be fundamentally repaired by a single solution.

“That is the same structure as Episode 1: not only CO2.”

> Yes.

> If you reject the single-villain theory, you must also reject the single-savior theory.

I laughed despite myself.

“After the CO2 single-villain theory comes the technology single-savior theory.”

> Humans like single culprits and single heroes.

“Because stories are easier that way.”

Forests.

Microorganisms.

Grasses.

Humans.

Plankton.

Earth is not moved by one enemy and one hero.

Everything is connected, breaking, and trying to reconnect.

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Episode 11 showed the protagonist realizing, through a search result, that Blue Pulse was only one part of a larger structure: the Direct Planetary Cooling model.

Up to Episode 10, Blue Pulse combined OBS and UMC to intervene in ocean heat and circulation.

But that alone is not enough.

The core problem of global warming is not limited to the ocean. It extends to the air and surface, soil and microorganisms, forests and grasslands, desertified land, and the whole water and carbon circulation system.

The core structure the protagonist identifies in this episode is the following trinity:

Deep ocean aeration.

Restarting ocean circulation and restoring the ocean carbon sink.

Mist cooling.

Wide-area cooling through evaporative heat and reducing excessive heating of the sea surface and land surface.

Soil regeneration.

Restoring the terrestrial carbon sink through leaf mold and microorganisms.

These are not separate countermeasures.

They mean letting the sea breathe, cooling the air and surface, and returning life to the soil.

Only when these move together does a path appear toward restoring Earth’s cooling capacity and carbon fixation cycle.

Ecosystem recovery through diverse weeds and desert cooling and greening are not outside this trinity. They are practical applications of soil regeneration and mist cooling.

This story does not deny CO2 reduction.

CO2 reduction is necessary.

But it is the act of turning down the faucet.

What is also needed is to restart the ocean circulation pump, reduce excessive heat, and repair the damaged carbon storage tank.

In Episode 12, the story returns to the question from Episode 3.

Is Super El Niño just an unlucky year?

Or is it a compound sign of circulation disruption layered on an already warmed Earth system?

The protagonist will look again at the red compound-risk map and say:
