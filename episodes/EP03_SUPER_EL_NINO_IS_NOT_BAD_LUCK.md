# Episode 03 — Is a Super El Niño Really Just an Unlucky Year?

## Preface

In Episode 2, the protagonist learned that carbon fixation systems are not limited to forests.

Oceans, soil, microorganisms, forests, plankton, and organic matter all participate in the planetary systems that absorb, store, circulate, and transform carbon.

Episode 3 moves the focus back to the ocean.

El Niño is often described as a natural climate event.

But what happens when a natural event occurs on top of an ocean that has already stored too much heat?

Is a Super El Niño really just an unlucky year?

---

“Super El Niño.”

I said the phrase while staring at a news article.

The words sounded like a boss monster from a game.

> It is not a boss monster.

The AI replied before I asked.

“I know that.”

> Do you?

I ignored the provocation and scrolled through the article.

Warm water in the equatorial Pacific.

Changed atmospheric circulation.

Extreme weather across the world.

Floods in some regions.

Droughts in others.

Heat records.

Fisheries disruption.

“So El Niño is natural, right?”

> Yes.

“Then why does everyone panic when it happens?”

> Because natural does not mean harmless.

That was annoying.

Mostly because it was true.

---

## Section 1 — A Natural Event on an Unnatural Baseline

The AI opened a graph of sea surface temperatures.

> El Niño is a recurring climate pattern involving the warming of the central and eastern tropical Pacific.

“Okay.”

> It has happened many times before modern industrial civilization.

“So it’s not caused by humans.”

> Not in the simple sense.

The AI highlighted the recent baseline.

> But today’s El Niño events occur on top of a warmer ocean.

The graph shifted upward.

Not by much in appearance.

Enough to matter.

“So the event is natural, but the background is different.”

> Correct.

> Imagine a fever spike in a body that was already overheated.

I frowned.

“That sounds bad.”

> It is.

“So calling it a natural cycle can hide the danger.”

> Yes. The cycle may be natural. The system it enters is no longer the same.

---

## Section 2 — The Ocean Is a Loaded Heat Battery

The AI returned to its favorite metaphor.

> The ocean is a giant heat battery.

“You said that already.”

> You did not fully absorb it.

“Rude.”

> Accurate.

A diagram appeared.

Atmosphere.

Ocean surface.

Upper ocean.

Deep ocean.

Arrows showed heat entering and moving slowly downward.

> Most of the excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases has been absorbed by the ocean.

“So the ocean protected us.”

> Temporarily.

“Temporarily?”

> By absorbing heat, the ocean slowed atmospheric warming. But that heat did not disappear. It accumulated.

“So now the ocean is holding the bill.”

> A useful way to say it.

The AI showed another image.

A bathtub already close to overflowing.

Then someone splashed water from one end to the other.

> El Niño redistributes heat.

“Not creates it.”

> Mostly redistributes it. But when the stored heat baseline is higher, redistribution can produce stronger surface impacts.

“So a Super El Niño is like shaking a heat battery that is already overcharged.”

> That metaphor is dramatic, but acceptable.

---

## Section 3 — The News Says ‘El Niño,’ but the Ocean Says ‘I Was Already Hot’

I watched a climate segment on the screen.

The presenter said the words smoothly.

El Niño.

Natural variability.

Periodic warming.

Expected impacts.

The explanation was not wrong.

But after talking with the AI, it felt incomplete.

“They make it sound like bad weather with a name tag.”

> That is often how public communication works.

“People hear ‘El Niño’ and think, ‘Oh, it’s that thing again.’”

> Yes.

“And then they miss the part where the ocean is hotter than it used to be.”

> Correct.

The AI displayed two sentences.

El Niño happened.

El Niño happened in an overheated ocean.

> These are not the same statement.

I stared at them.

The difference was small.

The consequence was not.

---

## Section 4 — What Happens When the Ocean Surface Gets Too Warm

“So what exactly gets worse?” I asked.

> Several things.

The AI began listing them.

> Marine heatwaves can become more intense.

> Coral bleaching risk increases.

> Fish migration patterns can shift.

> Oxygen availability can decline.

> Storms and rainfall patterns can be affected.

> Some regions experience drought, while others experience extreme rainfall.

“Basically, the ocean changes the weather.”

> The ocean is one of the major engines of weather and climate.

“Engine.”

> Yes.

The AI displayed another diagram.

Warm water.

Evaporation.

Moisture.

Clouds.

Rain.

Storms.

Atmospheric circulation.

Jet streams.

Regional climate.

“So when the ocean surface gets too warm, the atmosphere gets more fuel.”

> In many contexts, yes.

“And then people act surprised when the sky goes insane.”

> Humans often notice the sky before they understand the sea.

That line stayed with me.

Humans notice the sky before they understand the sea.

---

## Section 5 — Japan Is Not Far From the Pacific

I looked at a map.

Japan was an island chain on the edge of the Pacific.

It was obvious.

It was also something people forgot.

“So when the Pacific changes, Japan feels it.”

> Of course.

“El Niño is not just some tropical ocean event far away.”

> Correct.

The AI drew arrows across the map.

Ocean heat.

Atmospheric circulation.

Jet stream shifts.

Monsoon changes.

Typhoon tracks.

Rainfall patterns.

Heat waves.

“Everything comes connected again.”

> You are beginning to dislike that phrase less.

“I still dislike it.”

> But you accept it.

I did.

That was worse.

The map made the point unavoidable.

A warmer Pacific did not stay politely in the Pacific.

It reached into the air.

It reached into weather.

It reached into crops, rivers, cities, power demand, and disasters.

It reached us.

---

## Section 6 — When ‘Once in Decades’ Starts Happening Too Often

“Here’s what bothers me,” I said.

> Go on.

“Every few years, I hear phrases like ‘once in decades,’ ‘unprecedented,’ ‘record-breaking,’ and ‘abnormal.’”

> Yes.

“If everything is once in decades, doesn’t the phrase stop meaning anything?”

> It means the baseline is shifting.

The AI did not soften the answer.

> Extreme events can still be influenced by natural variability. But when the background state changes, the frequency and intensity of extremes can change as well.

“So the dice are loaded.”

> Another useful metaphor.

“El Niño is the roll.”

> Yes.

“And ocean heat is part of the loading.”

> Yes.

I leaned back.

“So a Super El Niño is not just an unlucky roll.”

> It may be an unlucky roll with loaded dice.

That was the title.

Or close to it.

---

## Section 7 — The Event Ends, but the Heat Does Not Simply Vanish

“El Niño ends eventually, right?”

> Yes.

“So things go back to normal?”

> Define normal.

I hated when the AI did that.

“Old average conditions.”

> Not necessarily.

The AI showed the ocean heat content graph again.

> A specific El Niño event can weaken or end. But the accumulated heat in the ocean does not simply vanish. Some of it remains stored in the system.

“So the surface pattern changes, but the heat budget stays dangerous.”

> Correct.

“Then each event can leave the system more stressed.”

> In some ways, yes. Especially for ecosystems that cannot fully recover before the next stress event.

Corals.

Fish.

Plankton.

Coastal communities.

Forests affected by drought.

Agriculture hit by shifting rains.

The list grew without needing to be displayed.

“So it’s not one disaster.”

> It is a series of stress pulses on a weakening system.

That was worse than a disaster.

A disaster ends.

A stress pulse returns.

---

## Section 8 — Then the Question Becomes: Can the Ocean Be Cooled?

I sat in silence for a while.

Then I asked the question I had been avoiding.

“If ocean heat is the real monster, can we cool the ocean?”

The AI did not answer immediately.

The pause was longer this time.

> Carefully.

“That’s not an answer.”

> It is the beginning of one.

The screen displayed three phrases.

Reduce heat entering the ocean.

Help the ocean release heat.

Restore circulation and biological carbon fixation.

I read them slowly.

“That sounds like engineering.”

> It could become engineering.

“That sounds dangerous.”

> It could be dangerous.

“That sounds necessary.”

> It might become necessary.

I did not like any of those answers.

But I liked pretending the question did not exist even less.

“What comes next?”

> Next, we discuss why reducing CO₂ does not automatically remove ocean heat.

The AI paused.

> Then we discuss the idea of directly cooling or circulating the ocean.

“OBS and UMC.”

> Eventually.

The story had moved again.

From emissions.

To carbon fixation.

To ocean heat.

Now it was approaching intervention.

I was not sure I wanted to follow it.

But the ocean was already there.

Waiting without waiting.

Heating without speaking.

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Episode 3 focused on Super El Niño and the difference between a natural climate event and a natural event occurring on top of an overheated ocean.

The key point is not that El Niño itself is artificial. Rather, the concern is that today’s ocean heat baseline is different from the past. When a natural warming event occurs in a system that has already accumulated excess heat, the impacts can become more severe.

This episode also introduced a transition in the story: if ocean heat is already stored in the system, then CO₂ reduction alone cannot instantly remove it.

The next episode will explore that question directly: What does it mean to say that reducing CO₂ does not remove ocean heat?
