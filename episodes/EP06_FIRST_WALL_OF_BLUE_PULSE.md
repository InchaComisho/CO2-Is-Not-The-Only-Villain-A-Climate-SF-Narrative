# Episode 06 — The First Wall Faced by Those Who Tried to Cool the Sea

## Preface

In Episode 4, the story organized three additional layers beyond CO₂ reduction:

- reducing heat entering the ocean through UMC,
- mixing ocean heat through OBS,
- and helping the ocean release heat.

In Episode 5, OBS was explored more deeply as a speculative system for giving the ocean a kind of “CPR.”

Episode 6 begins a new worldline:

What if people actually tried to operate OBS and UMC?

What would they face first?

Not physics.

Humans.

---

“So.”

One night, a new message appeared in the AI chat window.

> Have you seen the public call from the International Ocean Climate Summit?

“Huh?”

The question came so abruptly that I answered like an idiot.

> Technical approaches to ocean heat and extreme weather are being requested.

“Wait.”

I opened a news site in a hurry.

“I haven’t heard anything about that.”

> You were only reading El Niño articles.

White letters appeared on the black screen.

> After repeated Super El Niño events and marine heatwaves, there is now movement toward discussing the ocean itself as an active part of climate response.

“Seriously?”

I scrolled down and found the headline.

International Ocean Climate Summit:
New technical proposals requested for ocean heat, acidification, and extreme weather.

“This is…”

> Yes.

The AI paused for half a second.

> It may be time to upgrade OBS and UMC from story material to proposal material.

“Are you actually serious?”

---

## Section 1 — Turning a Fictional Concept Into a Real Format

“But come on,” I said, reading the submission guidelines.

“These international calls are for universities, research institutes, companies, government teams — people with actual credentials.”

> If you are talking about formal authority, yes.

“But?”

> A first draft of an idea can be made by anyone.

“Meaning?”

> The OBS and UMC concepts you have been organizing through the story can be rewritten as a proposal-style document.

> Whether researchers or organizations choose to pick it up comes later.

“So I’m like a shadow scenario writer.”

> Something like that.

> You create a worldview and specification that real players could understand, even if they decide to reject it.

That was both flattering and terrifying.

---

## Section 2 — The Title: A Civilization That Reached Toward the Ocean’s Heart

“So,” I said, inhaling slowly.

“I already said the title last time.”

> A Civilization That Reached Toward the Ocean’s Heart.

“You remembered.”

> There was nothing to forget.

“Then Episode 6 begins the inside of that title.”

> Correct.

The AI began writing a longer block of text.

> This episode should show the first wall faced by people trying to propose OBS and UMC.

> Not technical details first, but the structure of consensus-building and opposition.

“Politics already?”

> To touch the ocean is to enter politics.

I wanted to argue.

I could not.

---

## Section 3 — Project Name: Blue Pulse

Several days later, the AI showed me a draft.

At the top was a project name.

Blue Pulse.

“Blue Pulse?”

> The pulse of a blue heart.

> OBS and UMC together attempt to restore the pulse of the ocean’s circulation.

“That is honestly pretty dramatic.”

> You dislike it?

“No. I hate that I like it.”

> Then use it.

The AI began writing the summary.

> Blue Pulse: a localized ocean circulation boost and surface cooling project intended to mitigate ocean heat stress and support carbon fixation.

“That sounds very official.”

> At the naming stage, sounding official is useful.

“The problem is the content.”

> Correct.

---

## Section 4 — The First Three Questions

> At an international meeting, the first questions will be predictable.

The AI listed them.

> First: does it actually work?

> Second: what are the risks?

> Third: how does the cost-effectiveness compare with CO₂ reduction and adaptation measures?

“That is painfully realistic.”

> Which is why the story needs people who challenge the proposal.

“People who challenge it?”

> Yes.

> A team of scientists and engineers trying to present Blue Pulse.

> Experts, politicians, and NGOs pressing them with hard questions.

> That structure must be shown.

“So we need characters.”

> Finally.

---

## Section 5 — An Ocean Physicist, an NGO, and an Island Representative

“Who should be the face of Blue Pulse?” I asked, opening a notes app.

> At least three perspectives are needed.

The AI listed them.

> An ocean physicist responsible for the OBS model and risk assessment.

> An engineer or entrepreneur who can discuss UMC, hardware, cost, and scaling.

> A representative from a small island nation already threatened by marine heatwaves and sea-level rise.

“The island representative feels important.”

> They are.

> Technical curiosity alone will not move an international meeting.

> The voice of a place that may be lost if nothing is done changes the weight of the room.

“Who opposes it?”

> A cautious negotiator from a major power.

> Some marine protection NGOs.

> Scientists who believe CO₂ reduction must remain the overwhelming priority.

“Marine protection NGOs might oppose it?”

> Of course. From their perspective, ‘do not interfere further with the ocean’ is a natural response.

---

## Section 6 — The First Meeting: The Presentation Is Interrupted

“Let’s write the scene.”

I built a stage in my head.

A glass-walled international conference hall.

A screen at the front displaying the words Blue Pulse.

On the stage stood a female ocean physicist.

For now, I named her Aoi.

> Today, we will present Blue Pulse, a localized ocean circulation boost and surface cooling project designed to mitigate ocean heat and support carbon fixation.

“I’d like to see something like this for real once.”

> Then we reproduce it in fiction.

The AI helped draft the presentation.

> Our proposal does not reject CO₂ reduction or adaptation measures.

> On the contrary, it assumes them as necessary.

> We propose an additional layer that directly addresses heat already stored in the ocean and the weakening of carbon fixation systems.

The room stayed quiet through that part.

Then came the problem.

> In Phase 1, several dozen OBS units would be deployed in selected areas of the Pacific—

“Excuse me.”

A hand rose from the front row.

The nameplate belonged to a major power.

> What exactly is OBS?

> Is this a marine engineering experiment, or a form of geoengineering?

The air in the room changed.

---

## Section 7 — The Label Changes the Table

“There it is,” I muttered. “The label problem.”

> Yes.

The AI’s tone became more careful.

> Once a project is labeled geoengineering, the discussion table changes.

> Ethics committees.

> Long-term impact assessment.

> Governance and responsibility frameworks.

> International rules.

“Those are not bad things.”

> No. They are necessary.

> But to a world already overwhelmed by emissions reduction and adaptation, they can look like another impossible burden.

“So the easiest decision becomes shelving it.”

> Correct.

In the story, Aoi answered.

> OBS is a concept for boosting localized circulation from the surface to several hundred meters below, using existing pump technology and bubble plume dynamics.

> The medium is air and water. It does not alter the planetary radiation balance in the way stratospheric interventions would.

> If a label is required, I would describe it as a lower-layer marine engineering intervention.

A small laugh moved through part of the room.

Not approval.

Not rejection.

Just recognition that the vocabulary itself was already a battlefield.

---

## Section 8 — The Island Representative Says One Quiet Sentence

Later in the presentation, the representative of a small island nation took the microphone.

For now, I named him Tua.

> I speak from a different position than many people in this room.

> In my country, over the last several years, we have watched homes washed away by marine heatwaves, storm surges, and coastal erosion.

> We know CO₂ reduction is necessary.

> But we also know that what is necessary is not always done in time.

The room became quiet.

> If technologies such as OBS and UMC could weaken marine heatwaves even partially, if they could buy time for millions of people to move, adapt, or survive, then I cannot say that waiting for perfect safety is the only ethical choice.

“Damn.”

I leaned back in my chair.

> This is not a question of waiting for zero risk.

The AI added softly.

> It is a question of what level of risk is acceptable in exchange for what level of avoided harm.

Ethics and politics.

Not machines.

That was the first wall.

---

## Section 9 — The Protagonist Is Still Watching From the Other Side of the Screen

“So,” I said, watching the fictional live stream on my screen, “where do I stand in this worldline?”

> For now, you are an outside observer and the author of the story.

The AI paused.

> But your perspective could be transplanted into someone within the story.

“Transplanted?”

> For example, an unaffiliated eccentric who first organized the causal structure behind OBS and UMC.

> Someone without formal authority, but with an unusually persistent way of seeing causal chains.

“That is basically me.”

> More or less.

I did not know whether to feel honored or exposed.

---

## Section 10 — Standing at the Entrance of a Worldline That Tries

The AI wrote the final outline for the episode.

> Episode 6 should end here.

> The presentation of Blue Pulse at an international meeting.

> Technical and ethical objections.

> The island representative’s statement.

> Your own uncertain position.

> With this, Episode 7 can begin with the first actual test.

“So Episode 6 is only the entrance.”

> Yes.

> What lies beyond the door can be written next.

I closed the live-stream window.

“Then Episode 6 is about the first wall faced by those who tried to cool the sea.”

> Good title.

The AI answered plainly.

> And Episode 7 should begin with the day the first OBS unit starts.

---

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Episode 6 introduced Blue Pulse, a fictional project that attempts to combine OBS and UMC as a speculative response to ocean heat and weakened carbon fixation systems.

The episode does not present Blue Pulse as an approved or proven solution. Instead, it shows the first wall such a proposal would likely face: governance, ethics, responsibility, public trust, cost, and the fear of interfering with the ocean.

The central question is not only whether the technology could work, but who has the authority to decide whether it should be tried.

The next episode moves from proposal to experiment: the day the first OBS unit starts operating.
