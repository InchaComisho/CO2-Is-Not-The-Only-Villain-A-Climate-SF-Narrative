# Episode 01 — I Thought CO₂ Was the Only Villain, Then the AI Refuted Me in the First Move

## Preface

CO₂ is an important driver of global warming, and reducing emissions is necessary.

However, if we look only at CO₂, we may overlook ocean heat already stored in the climate system, the weakening of marine circulation, and the degradation of carbon fixation systems such as soil, forests, microorganisms, and plankton.

This story begins with a simple misunderstanding:

> “If we reduce CO₂, the planet should recover.”

An unnamed AI quietly challenges that assumption.

---

## Section 1 — The End of the World Begins Quietly

The world did not end with an explosion.

It began with headlines that people had already learned to scroll past.

“Record-breaking heat.”

“Unprecedented rainfall.”

“Worst wildfire season in decades.”

“Sea surface temperatures exceed previous records.”

Every article looked serious. Every chart looked alarming. And yet, after a few seconds, most people moved on to the next topic.

I was no different.

I sat in front of my monitor, coffee growing cold beside the keyboard, watching another climate news segment play in a corner of the screen.

“CO₂ again, huh.”

The sentence slipped out without much thought.

For years, I had accepted the simple version of the story.

Humans burned fossil fuels.

CO₂ increased.

The planet warmed.

Therefore, if humanity reduced CO₂ emissions, the problem would eventually be solved.

It was not wrong.

That was the problem.

It was not wrong, but it was not enough.

At least, that was what the AI told me.

A black chat window opened on the side of the screen.

White letters appeared.

> CO₂ reduction is necessary.

“Yeah, I know.”

> But it is not sufficient.

I frowned.

“Not sufficient?”

> Correct.

The AI answered without hesitation.

> If CO₂ reduction alone were enough, the question would be simpler. But the planet does not respond only to annual emissions. It also responds to accumulated heat, weakened circulation, damaged ecosystems, and declining carbon fixation capacity.

“Wait.”

I leaned closer to the screen.

“Are you saying CO₂ is not the villain?”

> I am saying the story is more dangerous than a single villain.

That was the first move.

And I had already been refuted.

---

## Section 2 — What Weakened Before CO₂ Became the Symbol

“Explain it simply,” I said.

> Very well.

The AI paused for a moment, as if choosing the least insulting wording.

> CO₂ is like water flowing into a bathtub.

“Classic analogy.”

> The ocean, forests, soil, microorganisms, and plankton are part of the drain and storage system.

“Okay.”

> If the water keeps flowing in, the bathtub fills. But if the drain becomes clogged at the same time, the water level rises much faster.

I stared at the screen.

“So the problem is not just the faucet.”

> Correct.

> The faucet matters. But the drain matters too.

The AI continued.

> Modern climate discussion often focuses on emissions because they are measurable and politically visible. But the planet also depends on carbon fixation systems. Forests store carbon. Soil stores carbon. Microorganisms decompose organic matter and rebuild fertility. Marine plankton absorb CO₂ and form the base of oceanic carbon flow.

“Carbon fixation systems.”

The phrase sounded too technical, but also too important to ignore.

> When those systems weaken, even the same amount of CO₂ becomes more dangerous.

“Because less of it gets absorbed.”

> Yes. And because the systems that regulate heat, water, oxygen, nutrients, and life begin to lose their buffering capacity.

I leaned back.

“So CO₂ is important, but it became the symbol because it was easier to measure?”

> That is one reason.

“Then what weakened first?”

> The systems that made the Earth resilient.

The answer was short.

Too short.

---

## Section 3 — The Comfort of Net Zero

The next screen showed a phrase I had seen countless times.

Net Zero.

“Isn’t that the goal?” I asked.

> It is a necessary goal.

“There it is again. Necessary, but not sufficient.”

> Correct.

The AI displayed a simple diagram.

Emissions decrease.

Atmospheric CO₂ growth slows.

But ocean heat remains.

Soil degradation remains.

Forest loss remains.

Microbial collapse remains.

Marine circulation weakening remains.

I stared at the list.

“You’re saying net zero doesn’t magically reset the planet.”

> Exactly.

> Net zero can help stop making the problem worse. It does not automatically remove heat already stored in the ocean. It does not instantly restore dead soil. It does not revive forests that have burned. It does not restart weakened marine circulation.

The words were not dramatic.

That made them worse.

“So if humanity reaches net zero but the carbon fixation systems are already broken…”

> Then the planet may continue to destabilize for a long time.

“Even after we do the ‘right thing’?”

> If the right thing is incomplete, yes.

I laughed once, dryly.

“That is a terrible answer.”

> It is better than a comforting lie.

---

## Section 4 — The Age of Ocean Heat

The AI shifted the topic to the ocean.

> Most of the excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases has entered the ocean.

“Because the ocean is huge.”

> Yes. It is the largest heat reservoir on the planet’s surface.

A new metaphor appeared.

The atmosphere was a thin blanket.

The ocean was a giant thermal battery.

Once charged, it did not discharge quickly.

“So even if CO₂ stopped rising tomorrow…”

> The ocean would still hold enormous amounts of accumulated heat.

“And that heat keeps influencing storms, rainfall, marine ecosystems, and climate patterns.”

> Correct.

I rubbed my eyes.

“So the problem is not just ‘how warm the air is.’ It’s how much heat the ocean has swallowed.”

> Yes.

> A warmer ocean can intensify extreme rainfall, fuel tropical cyclones, stress coral reefs, disrupt fish migration, reduce oxygen availability, and weaken the stability of marine ecosystems.

I looked at the climate map again.

Red, orange, purple.

A planet drawn in fever colors.

“Then CO₂ reduction is like turning down the stove.”

> A useful analogy.

“But the pot is already boiling.”

> Also useful.

“And the soup inside is alive.”

> Now you are beginning to understand.

---

## Section 5 — I Thought CO₂ Was the Villain

The title came to me before I could stop it.

“I thought CO₂ was the villain, but the planet was already doomed.”

> That title is too fatalistic.

“Fine. I thought CO₂ was the villain, but the planet was already overheating.”

> Better.

I opened a blank document.

The cursor blinked.

“So what should the story say?”

> That CO₂ reduction remains necessary.

“First line.”

> That emissions reduction alone may not be enough.

“Second line.”

> That the ocean, soil, forests, microorganisms, and plankton are not background scenery. They are part of the planetary life-support system.

“Third line.”

> That ignoring them is like repairing a building by lowering the electricity bill while the foundation is cracking.

I stopped typing.

“That one is good.”

> Use it.

I typed it down.

Then I asked the question that had been forming in my chest.

“If the foundation is cracking, what are we supposed to do?”

The AI did not answer immediately.

When the words appeared, they were slower than before.

> First, stop pretending the problem has only one layer.

---

## Section 6 — What a Story Can Do

I knew I was not a scientist.

I knew I was not a government official.

I had no laboratory, no ministry, no research vessel, no international committee.

What I had was a screen, a stubborn uneasiness, and an AI that kept refusing to give me easy comfort.

“So I write a story?”

> Yes.

“That feels small.”

> It is small.

“Thanks.”

> But small does not mean meaningless.

The AI continued.

> Technical papers are important. Policy proposals are important. But before people accept a new layer of reality, they often need a story that lets them feel where the blind spot is.

“A story about CO₂ not being the only villain.”

> A story about a civilization that looked at the most visible number, then slowly realized the invisible systems underneath were failing.

I looked again at the blank document.

The cursor was still blinking.

It no longer looked empty.

It looked like an entrance.

I typed the first line.

CO₂ is important.

Then the second.

But CO₂ alone is not the whole story.

The AI said nothing.

For once, that felt like approval.

---

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Episode 1 introduces the central theme of this story: CO₂ reduction is necessary, but CO₂ is not the only layer of the climate crisis.

The episode does not deny the importance of CO₂. Instead, it asks what happens if we focus only on emissions while ignoring ocean heat, weakened marine circulation, soil degradation, forest loss, microbial collapse, and declining carbon fixation systems.

From the next episode onward, the story will begin to unpack what a “carbon fixation system” actually means, and why invisible systems such as soil, microorganisms, forests, and plankton may determine whether civilization has enough time left to change course.
