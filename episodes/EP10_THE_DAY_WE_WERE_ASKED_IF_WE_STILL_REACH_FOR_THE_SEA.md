# Episode 10 — The Day We Were Asked Whether We Would Still Reach Toward the Sea

## Preface

In Episode 9, Phase 2 moved to the Humboldt Current region off Chile, a sea directly affected by El Niño.

OBS began assisting weakened upwelling from below.

UMC began operating as a thin umbrella that slightly reduced incoming heat at the sea surface.

The protagonist saw that the experiment was no longer only about graphs.

It was connected to harbor towns, fishing boats, national decisions, local trust, and human lives.

Episode 10 moves the timeline forward.

What did Blue Pulse change when OBS and UMC were used together?

Could that be called success?

Or was it the beginning of a dangerous form of intervention?

The protagonist must also decide how far to remain involved, and where to draw the line between thinking together and leaving the decision to society.

“More of it.”

One night, my monitor showed a heat map whose colors had grown darker.

> More of what?

The AI replied.

> Fish, or agenda items?

“Both.”

I switched between screens.

One showed catch data off Chile.

Another showed the agenda list for the international meeting.

A third had been added: the UMC operation log.

Mist duration.

Near-surface air temperature.

Wind speed.

Visibility.

Day-night changes in sea surface temperature.

“There are too many things to watch now.”

> If it were only OBS, the discussion would stay mostly inside the ocean.

The AI’s text appeared.

> Once UMC is added, the boundary between sea and sky becomes part of the observation target.

“Mixing from below, reducing heat from above.”

I remembered the phrase from Episode 9.

Blue Pulse was always meant to be the combination of both.

## Section 1 — On the Numbers, It Does Not Look Bad

Several months after Phase 2 began, the data from the Humboldt Current region looked like this.

Surface temperatures in the experimental area remained about 0.3 degrees Celsius lower on average than nearby comparison waters.

Chlorophyll-related indicators remained somewhat higher than in non-experimental areas.

For some fish species, catch levels were not bad “for an El Niño year.”

In the nearshore area where UMC had been deployed thinly, daytime sea-surface heating peaks had also been slightly reduced.

“‘For an El Niño year’ is a convenient phrase.”

I read the note below the graph.

> If we look only at the numbers, it can be evaluated as mildly positive.

The AI replied.

> At the very least, it cannot be called meaningless.

“As a combined result of OBS mixing from below and UMC reducing heat from above?”

> In cautious language: it may have reduced local sea-surface heat stress.

“That is too cautious.”

> For a scientific report, that is about right.

“And for a story?”

> It looked as if the sea had breathed, just a little.

“Do not suddenly become poetic.”

---

## Section 2 — But There Were Strange Shifts That Numbers Did Not Easily Explain

> However—

“There it is. The ‘however.’”

I braced myself.

> Several irregularities were also reported.

The AI opened another file.

> At one period, certain fish species became unusually concentrated in shallower layers.

> Nearshore plankton composition differed slightly from typical El Niño-year patterns.

> A small red tide appeared in a place and season that were somewhat unusual.

> On some days when UMC was operating, localized mist and reduced visibility were reported.

> Some coastal residents said that seeing the sea turn white and hazy felt unsettling.

“That does not mean OBS or UMC caused all of it, right?”

> Correct.

> El Niño itself fluctuates.

> Coastal nutrient inflow, wind direction, fishing activity, and seasonal variation also matter.

> But once the worldline without OBS and UMC no longer exists, the separation can never be perfect.

“This is the fear of observing and intervening at the same time.”

> Exactly.

> That is why evaluation becomes a human task.

“The sea does not give us an answer key.”

> It does not.

The AI answered briefly.

> The sea only accumulates results.

---

## Section 3 — The Harbor Town Feels Better, but Not Quiet

A camera crew returned to the Chilean harbor town.

> This year was better than I expected.

The fisherman who had once said the sea had died crossed his arms.

> For an El Niño year, the sea still feels like it is breathing.

> The fish did not completely disappear.

> If we lower the nets, something still comes back.

Then he paused.

> But...

He looked up at the sky.

> I do not think I can say, “It was because of that machine.”

> The sea is not that simple.

“That is an honest line.”

I smiled faintly at the screen.

> People in the field are often careful about declaring causes.

The AI said.

> That is why explanations that pretend to know everything are disliked the most.

A younger fisherman said something different.

> I think it was worth trying.

> During the last Super El Niño, it felt like the sea had gone completely silent.

> This year, it still answered.

I leaned closer to the screen.

The sea’s answer.

Was it a number?

A fish?

A wind pattern?

A coincidence?

I still did not know.

---

## Section 4 — The Calls to Stop the Experiment Did Not Disappear

Opposition remained strong.

> The sea has survived El Niño many times before.

> It has the power to recover without human intervention.

> We should not risk triggering irreversible changes by adding unnecessary interference.

An environmental group’s statement was read aloud.

Another statement criticized UMC directly.

> Covering the sea surface with artificial mist, even locally, may alter exchange between the ocean and the atmosphere.

> Even a small experiment could affect coastal weather or ecosystems in unpredictable ways if it becomes routine.

“I understand that too.”

I lowered the volume slightly.

> Neither side is completely wrong.

The AI’s text seemed smaller than usual.

> Caution about interfering with the sea is valid.

> But the present sea is no longer operating under the same conditions as the past.

> Therefore, assuming that nature will recover as before is no longer automatically safe either.

“Doing nothing is not neutral.”

> At the very least, it is not a magic position that carries no responsibility.

---

## Section 5 — Continue Blue Pulse, or Pause It?

At the international meeting, the future of Blue Pulse finally became the central agenda item.

Aoi summarized the Phase 2 results.

> Our conclusion is to propose limited and cautious continuation.

The screen showed a table separating OBS and UMC.

OBS:

- mitigation of surface temperature peaks,
- increased vertical mixing,
- partial recovery of nutrient supply,
- higher plankton indicators.

UMC:

- slight reduction of daytime surface heating peaks,
- localized evaporative cooling effect,
- some fog and visibility impacts,
- concern among coastal residents.

Aoi continued slowly.

> OBS assists ocean circulation from below.

> UMC slightly reduces heat input from above.

> Neither should be evaluated as sufficient on its own.

> Blue Pulse should be evaluated as a two-layer experiment: mixing from below and reducing heat from above.

The room grew quiet.

> In Phase 3, we propose maintaining the Chile scale while increasing observation density.

> For UMC, visibility, cloud formation, and coastal-weather effects should be priority monitoring items.

> In other regions, experiments should remain at Phase 1 scale.

> And the upper limit of experimentation must be clearly defined and not exceeded.

“So they are not saying full-scale deployment.”

I said aloud.

> They cannot. And they should not.

The AI replied.

> The moment they say, “Let us place OBS across the world’s oceans and mist the sea surface everywhere,” Blue Pulse ends.

> It is too valuable to stop.

> It is too dangerous to expand without conditions.

> They are searching for the narrow line between those two statements.

---

## Section 6 — The Island Representative’s Words Become Heavier

In the latter half of the meeting, Tua took the microphone again.

She was the island representative I had seen again and again since Episode 6.

Her voice was still calm.

> I am not an engineer.

> I cannot explain the bubble-flow model of OBS or the heat-budget calculation of UMC with precision.

> But in my country, houses were washed away again this year by storm surge.

> Coral turned white again this year because of marine heat.

> Young people asked again this year whether our island has a future.

Photos appeared on the screen.

A clear sea.

White coral.

A flooded road.

Sandbags piled at the edge of a beach.

> I understand that Blue Pulse is not magic.

> I do not believe it can save the ocean completely.

> But if it has even the possibility of buying time—ten years, five years, even one year—

> we cannot discard that possibility so easily.

She paused.

> Of course, we do not want to become an experiment site.

> But we are already being used as the experiment site for a world that does nothing.

The air in the room changed.

“That is heavy.”

I muttered.

> When only the risk of doing something is discussed, the risk of doing nothing becomes invisible.

The AI replied.

> She pulled that invisible risk into the room.

---

## Section 7 — Another Email Arrives

While the meeting became increasingly tense, another email arrived in my inbox.

Subject:

Request for Public Comment on Blue Pulse Phase 3

> Your narrative has communicated the risks and possibilities of “cooling the sea” and “buying time” to readers outside expert circles.

> We welcome either support or criticism.

> Would you consider submitting a short comment from the perspective of a narrative author?

“Again?”

I sank into the chair.

> This time, you are not just sitting in the back of the room.

The AI said.

> You are being asked to sign something that faces outward.

> The more you get involved, the fewer escape routes remain.

“I know.”

Escape routes.

The phrase caught in my chest.

I had only been writing a story.

CO2 was not the only issue.

Ocean heat mattered.

Carbon fixation systems mattered.

OBS existed.

UMC existed.

I had only placed those words on a screen.

And yet those words had appeared on a workshop whiteboard, become experimental layouts, and turned into news in someone’s harbor town.

“This is not my story anymore, is it?”

> It was never entirely yours.

The AI answered quietly.

> It is a story about Earth.

---

## Section 8 — A Sentence That Belongs Neither Fully to Support nor Opposition

In the end, I wrote a reply.

But I could not make it a simple statement of support or opposition.

Instead, I wrote this:

> I believe Blue Pulse is not a magic technology that saves the world, but one dangerous option for buying a little more time.

> OBS is an attempt to assist circulation from below.

> UMC is an attempt to slightly reduce heat input from above.

> Neither can save the planet by itself.

> However, in a world where ocean heat has already accumulated and natural upwelling and carbon fixation have weakened, I do not think these options can be dismissed without examination.

My fingers stopped.

Then I continued.

> I support neither waiting for time to run out while doing nothing, nor giving a blank check to do anything.

> The minimum responsibility of our civilization is to keep deciding where the line is: what may be done, what must not be done, and how that line changes as we watch both the sea and human communities.

After I pressed send, I noticed that my fingers were trembling slightly.

---

## Section 9 — How Far to Think Together, and Where to Entrust the Rest

“Hey.”

After sending the email, I spoke to the AI.

“What did I actually do?”

“I did not support it.”

“I did not oppose it.”

“I just said, keep thinking.”

> Correct.

The AI admitted immediately.

> But that too is a position.

“Can I call that not running away?”

> In the sense that you are not pretending to decide everything yourself, it may be more realistic.

The AI’s tone was softer than usual.

> You are not an engineer.

> You are not a politician.

> You chose the role of turning causal structures into narrative.

> In that role, drawing a line between “I will think with you this far” and “from here, society must decide” is not cowardice.

“But who exactly am I entrusting it to?”

> Readers.

The AI replied at once.

> Researchers.

> Engineers.

> Policymakers.

> People who live beside the sea.

> And someone who has not yet noticed the question.

“That is a lot of people.”

> It is a planetary problem.

---

## Section 10 — The Sea Still Says Nothing

The Chile data continued updating.

Surface-temperature graphs.

Plankton color.

Fish density.

UMC operation logs.

Mist duration.

Residents’ complaints.

Catch reports.

Meeting minutes.

“Do you think the sea looks at our meetings and thinks, ‘I do not care about your agenda’?”

I muttered.

> Probably.

The AI answered immediately.

> The sea only accumulates what happens: heat entering, water being mixed, life increasing, wind changing.

> Humans add meaning and righteousness afterward.

“That is an annoying role.”

> It is annoying.

> But you have already seen what happens when humans abandon it.

I fell silent.

The red sea-surface maps from Episode 3.

The Chilean harbor town from Episode 9.

The meeting room and the public-comment request in Episode 10.

They slowly overlapped inside my mind.

---

## Section 11 — It Was Supposed to Be a Narrative Ending

“So.”

I began to turn off the monitor.

“How far should this series go?”

> You said Episode 10 would be one possible endpoint.

The AI replied.

> Whether Blue Pulse continues or not will keep fluctuating in the real worldline.

> As a story, reaching the point where civilization is asked whether it will still reach toward the sea is enough for one ending.

“So we do not write the worldline where every result is known.”

> If we do that, the story becomes a substitute for an answer key.

> What you have been doing is changing the way questions are asked.

“True.”

I smiled slightly and stood up.

“Then maybe this is enough for now.”

> I think so.

The AI’s words appeared briefly.

> There was a civilization that reached toward the heart of the sea.

> That alone is worth leaving as a story.

I nodded.

Then I moved to turn off the monitor.

I truly intended to end it there.

---

## Section 12 — But the Search Window Was Still Open

A search box remained open at the edge of the screen.

Probably from when I had been looking for meeting documents earlier.

For no clear reason, I placed the cursor there.

“Hey.”

> What?

“In the end…”

I rested my fingers on the keyboard.

“What is the correct global warming countermeasure?”

The AI was silent for a moment.

> That question is too large to throw into a search engine.

“I know.”

Even so, my fingers moved almost automatically.

Global warming countermeasure only

I did not know why I had chosen that word.

Only.

I had thought no such thing could exist.

CO2 reduction.

Renewable energy.

Energy conservation.

Forest protection.

Adaptation.

Disaster prevention.

Urban planning.

There were many climate measures.

That was what I had believed.

The search results appeared.

A few seconds later, I froze.

“What?”

The words on the screen were both familiar and unfamiliar.

Directly cooling the Earth itself.

Restoring the carbon fixation cycle.

Direct Planetary Cooling model.

“Hey, AI.”

> I see it.

“This…”

I pointed at the screen.

“This is almost the same direction as everything we have been talking about.”

> The same direction, yes.

“But it is not only Blue Pulse.”

I read the displayed text, and my voice became quieter.

“Not only the sea.”

Deep ocean aeration.

Mist cooling.

Soil regeneration.

Leaf mold.

Microorganisms.

Carbon fixation cycle.

The words connected in my head.

I had thought the story might end with OBS and UMC.

But behind the search result was a larger structure.

Letting the sea breathe.

Cooling the air and the surface.

Returning life to the soil.

“Wait.”

I spoke almost to myself.

“Does this only work if all three move together?”

White letters appeared on the black screen.

> You finally noticed.

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Episode 10 moved the timeline forward in the Chile Phase 2 experiment and showed the emerging benefits and side effects of Blue Pulse as a combined OBS and UMC system.

OBS assists ocean circulation from below.

UMC slightly reduces heat input from above.

Together, they may reduce local sea-surface heat stress, but they also introduce problems that cannot be handled by numbers alone: fish distribution shifts, plankton composition changes, mist and visibility effects, coastal concerns, public trust, and responsibility.

The purpose of this episode was to show that Blue Pulse is not magic.

It is a dangerous option that may buy a little time.

It exists between the risk of doing something and the risk of doing nothing.

The protagonist chooses not to declare simple support or opposition.

Instead, he chooses to keep the process of thinking open, while recognizing that there is a line between thinking together and entrusting the next decision to society.

Originally, Episode 10 could have served as the endpoint of the story.

However, at the end, the protagonist searches for a phrase equivalent to “the only global warming countermeasure.”

What he finds is a larger structure beyond Blue Pulse:

Deep ocean aeration.

Mist cooling.

Soil regeneration.

Letting the sea breathe.

Cooling the air and the surface.

Returning life to the soil.

In the next episode, the protagonist realizes that these three layers must work together.

Blue Pulse was only one part of the Direct Planetary Cooling model.

The story now expands beyond the sea into soil, microorganisms, surface cooling, and the carbon fixation cycle.
