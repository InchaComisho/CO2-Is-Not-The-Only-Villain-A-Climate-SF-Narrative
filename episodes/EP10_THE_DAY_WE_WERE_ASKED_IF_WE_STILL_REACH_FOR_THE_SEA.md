# Episode 10 — The Day We Were Asked Whether We Would Still Reach Toward the Sea

## Preface

In Episode 9, Phase 2 moved to the Humboldt Current region off Chile.

OBS and UMC began operating in a sea stressed by El Niño.

For the first time, the protagonist saw that the data were no longer only numbers.

They were connected to harbor towns, fishing boats, local trust, and human lives.

Episode 10 moves the timeline forward.

Benefits and side effects begin to take clearer shape.

Blue Pulse must decide whether to continue, pause, or remain only an experiment.

And the protagonist must decide how far to stay involved.

---

“It increased.”

One night, a darker heat map filled my monitor.

> Which one?

The AI asked.

> Fish, or agenda items?

“Both.”

I switched the display.

One screen showed fishery data off Chile.

The other showed the agenda for the next international meeting.

---

## Section 1 — On the Numbers, It Is Not Bad

Several months after Phase 2 began, the data from the Humboldt Current region looked like this:

Surface temperatures in the test area remained about 0.3 degrees Celsius lower on average than nearby non-test areas.

Phytoplankton indicators remained somewhat higher and more stable.

For some fish species, catches stayed at a level that was not bad for an El Niño year.

“‘For an El Niño year’ is a convenient phrase.”

I read the annotation on the graph.

> If we look only at the numbers, it is reasonable to call the result slightly positive.

The AI answered calmly.

> At the very least, it cannot be dismissed as meaningless.

> However—

“There it is.”

“The however.”

---

## Section 2 — Strange Shifts That Numbers Do Not Capture Cleanly

> Several irregularities have also been reported.

The AI opened another document.

> During one period, certain fish species concentrated too strongly in shallower layers.

> The plankton community near the coast differed slightly from typical El Niño-year patterns.

> A small red tide appeared in a place and season that were somewhat unusual.

“That doesn’t mean OBS or UMC caused all of it.”

> Correct.

> El Niño itself has variability.

> But because the worldline without OBS and UMC no longer exists, perfect separation is impossible.

“Observation and intervention are happening at the same time.”

> Yes.

> That is why evaluation becomes a human responsibility.

The graphs were not lying.

But they were not explaining themselves either.

---

## Section 3 — The Harbor Mood Is Not Bad, But It Is Not Calm

A camera returned to the Chilean port town.

> This year was better than expected.

The same fisherman who once said the sea had died spoke with his arms crossed.

> For an El Niño year, the sea still feels like it is breathing.

> But…

He looked at the sky for a moment.

> Saying it was all because of that experiment feels wrong too.

> The sea is not that simple.

“That is a painfully honest line.”

I smiled at the screen.

> People closest to the sea often hesitate to declare a single cause.

The AI said.

> That is why explanations pretending to know everything are distrusted.

---

## Section 4 — The Call to Stop the Experiment Has Not Disappeared

Opposition remained strong.

> The ocean has survived El Niño events many times before.

> It has its own recovery power.

> We should not risk irreversible changes by interfering unnecessarily.

A statement from an environmental organization was read aloud.

“I understand that too.”

I lowered the volume slightly.

> Neither side is completely wrong.

The AI’s text seemed smaller than usual.

> But today’s ocean is no longer operating under the same conditions as the past.

> Therefore, the assumption that leaving it alone is automatically safer can no longer be accepted without question.

That was the problem.

The old categories had stopped working.

---

## Section 5 — Continue Blue Pulse, or Pause It?

At the international meeting, Blue Pulse finally became a formal agenda item again.

Aoi summarized the Phase 2 results.

> Our conclusion is to recommend limited and cautious continuation.

> In Phase 3, the Chile test area should remain at its current scale while observation density is increased.

> Other regions should remain at Phase 1-scale trials only.

> A clear upper boundary must be defined before any larger deployment is considered.

“So they’re not calling for full-scale deployment.”

> They cannot, and they should not.

The AI answered.

> The moment Blue Pulse says, ‘Let us cover the world’s oceans with OBS units,’ the project is over.

> They are searching for the narrow line between too valuable to stop and too dangerous to expand freely.

That line was thin.

And everyone could see it.

---

## Section 6 — Another Email Arrives

While the meeting continued, another email appeared in my inbox.

Subject:

Request for Public Comment on Blue Pulse Phase 3

The message read:

> Your narrative has helped communicate the risks and possibilities of the idea of cooling the ocean and buying time.

> Whether you support or oppose Phase 3, we would like to receive a short comment from the perspective of the author of the story.

“Again?”

I sank into the chair.

> This time you are not sitting in the back of the room.

The AI said.

> You are being asked to sign something that faces outward.

“The more involved I get, the fewer places there are to hide.”

> Yes.

“I know.”

I did know.

That did not make it easier.

---

## Section 7 — A Sentence That Joins Neither Support Nor Opposition

In the end, I wrote a response.

But I could not write a simple statement of support or opposition.

Instead, I wrote this:

> I believe Blue Pulse is not a magic solution that saves the world.

> It is one dangerous option for buying a little more time.

> I do not support waiting passively until time runs out.

> I also do not support giving blank permission to do anything in the name of emergency.

> The minimum responsibility of civilization is to continue drawing and redrawing the line between what may be tried and what must not be exceeded, while watching both the sea and the people who depend on it.

> The process of thinking must not be stopped.

After pressing send, I noticed that my finger was trembling slightly.

---

## Section 8 — A Conversation About Where to Stop

“Hey.”

After sending the comment, I spoke to the AI.

“What I just did was basically refuse to say yes or no.”

> Yes.

The AI admitted it immediately.

> But that is also a choice.

“Can I call that not running away?”

> In this case, yes.

“How?”

> Because you are not trying to decide everything alone.

The AI’s tone was unusually soft.

> You are not an engineer in charge of the system.

> You are not a negotiator representing a state.

> You chose the role of making causal relationships visible through story.

> Drawing a line between ‘I will help think this far’ and ‘beyond this point, others must decide and take responsibility’ is not evasion. It can be honesty.

I read the message twice.

I wanted it to be true.

Maybe wanting that was also part of the burden.

---

## Section 9 — The Sea Still Says Nothing

The Chile data continued to update.

Surface temperature graphs.

Plankton color.

Fish density.

“Sometimes I think the ocean must be looking at us like it has no interest in our agenda.”

> It probably is.

The AI answered immediately.

> The sea simply accumulates what enters it: heat, mixed water, nutrients, living things, dead things, human intervention, and the consequences of non-intervention.

> Humans arrive later and attach meaning, correctness, success, or failure.

“What an annoying role.”

> Annoying, yes.

> But you have already seen what happens when humans abandon that role.

The sea did not speak.

The graphs did.

But only in a language humans had to interpret.

---

## Section 10 — A Narrative Pause

“So,” I said, turning off the monitor, “how far should this series go?”

> You said ten episodes.

The AI replied.

> Whether Blue Pulse continues will remain uncertain in the real worldline of the story.

> As a narrative, it is enough to show that there was a civilization that decided to try.

“So we do not write a worldline where all results are known.”

> If you do, the story becomes a substitute for an answer key.

> What you have been doing is changing the way the question is held.

I smiled a little.

“That’s true.”

I stood from the chair.

“Then this is a good place to pause.”

> I agree.

The AI’s final lines appeared.

> There was a civilization that reached toward the ocean’s heart.

> That alone is worth leaving as a story.

---

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Episode 10 moved the Blue Pulse Phase 2 timeline forward.

Some benefits became clearer: slight local surface cooling, more stable plankton indicators, and less severe fishery collapse than feared in parts of the test area.

At the same time, side effects and uncertainties also became clearer: shifts in species distribution, unusual plankton composition, and the impossibility of perfectly separating the effects of El Niño from the effects of intervention.

The protagonist ultimately refuses both simple support and simple opposition. Instead, he argues that the process of thinking, monitoring, limiting, and revising must not stop.

Across the series, the central message is this:

CO₂ reduction remains necessary, but it may not be sufficient by itself if ocean heat, weakened circulation, carbon fixation decline, soil degradation, forest loss, and microbial collapse are ignored.

Cooling, mixing, and buying time are not magic solutions. They are risky options that may exist between doing nothing and doing anything.

The story ends here as a narrative pause, not as a final answer.

The questions remain:

How far should civilization intervene?

Where must it stop?

How much risk does doing nothing also contain?

And who gets to decide before the sea answers in its own slow language?
