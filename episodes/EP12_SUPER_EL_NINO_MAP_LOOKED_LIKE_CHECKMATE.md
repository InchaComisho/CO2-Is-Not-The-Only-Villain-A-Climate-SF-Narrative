Deep ocean aeration.

Mist cooling.

Soil regeneration.

Letting the sea breathe, cooling the air and surface, and returning life to the soil.

## Section 1 — El Niño Was Not Just About Sea Surface Temperature

“El Niño…”

I read the beginning of the article.

“I used to think of it as a phenomenon where the Pacific Ocean gets warmer.”

> Many people understand it that way.

“The sea near the equator gets warmer.”

“Trade winds weaken.”

“Japan may get heatwaves or heavy rain.”

“Something like that, right?”

> Not wrong.

The AI did not begin by denying it.

> But not enough.

“There it is again.”

> It has been the same since Episode 1.

> Not wrong, but not enough.

I laughed slightly.

That sentence almost described the whole series.

The AI continued.

> El Niño is not only a sea-surface-temperature event.

> Upwelling weakens.

> Nutrients rise less easily.

> Phytoplankton decline.

> Fish decline.

> Rainfall locations shift.

> Some regions dry.

> Wildfire risk increases.

> Soil moisture is lost.

> Agriculture and fisheries both become unstable.

“So…”

I spoke slowly.

“It is not just an event where the ocean gets warmer. It is an event where Earth’s circulation shifts.”

> Yes.

> Heat, water, nutrients, oxygen, life, and carbon are shaken together.

## Section 2 — When I Had the AI Read the English GitHub Article

“This GitHub article is in English.”

I passed the URL to the AI.

“Can you read it?”

> Of course.

The AI answered immediately.

> The title is: El Niño, Super El Niño, Global Warming, and Ecosystem Disruption.

“El Niño, Super El Niño, global warming, and ecosystem disruption.”

> Yes.

> The core of the article is to treat El Niño not merely as a weather event, but as a compound risk occurring on an already warmed Earth.

“Compound risk.”

> Modern El Niño can be described like this.

```text
Modern El Niño impact
= Natural ENSO variability
+ Anthropogenic warming baseline
+ Ecosystem vulnerability
```

“A natural phenomenon, plus warming, plus ecosystem degradation.”

> Yes.

> In the past, El Niño was a natural fluctuation occurring on a more stable Earth system.

> But now the situation is different.

> The ocean is already warmer.

> Soil dries more easily.

> Forests burn more easily.

> Coral bleaches more easily.

> Agriculture is more vulnerable to heatwaves and heavy rainfall.

> Then El Niño arrives on top of that.

I was silent for a moment.

“That is… plainly bad.”

> Plainly bad.

The AI’s answer was almost too calm.

The AI summarized part of the article.

```text
Heat circulation       Redistribution of heat
Water circulation      Rainfall and evaporation patterns
Nutrient circulation   Nutrient supply from deep water to the surface
Oxygen circulation     Marine respiration and ecosystem balance
Biological circulation Energy movement through food webs
Carbon circulation     Biological carbon uptake and storage
```

“This is almost a list of Earth’s organs.”

I said before thinking.

> Close.

The AI replied briefly.

> The ocean is lung, heart, and stomach.

> Forests are lung, skin, and storage.

> Soil is intestine, memory device, and carbon warehouse.

“That became strangely biological.”

> If you see Earth as living circulation, that is appropriate.

I looked at the categories again.

Heat.

Water.

Nutrients.

Oxygen.

Biology.

Carbon.

Until now, I had thought of them as separate problems.

Heat was global warming.

Water was drought or heavy rain.

Nutrients were marine ecosystems.

Oxygen was hypoxic zones.

Biology was biodiversity.

Carbon was CO2.

But El Niño shakes them together.

“They are not breaking one by one.”

> Correct.

> They are connected, and weakening together.

“That is why it is frightening.”

> That is why it is frightening.

> It is too early to declare checkmate.

The AI answered immediately.

“But look at this map.”

“The Pacific is under heat stress.”

“The Indian Ocean is warmer.”

“Africa has drought.”

“Australia has drought.”

“South America has heavy rain.”

“North America has jet-stream shifts and heat.”

“Coral reefs bleach.”

“Where are we supposed to run?”

> This is not a map of escape routes.

The AI’s words moved quietly.

> It is a map of connected risks.

“Connected risks.”

> Yes.

> Pacific heat changes atmospheric circulation.

> Atmospheric circulation changes where rain falls.

> Rainfall shifts produce droughts and floods.

> Drought weakens forests and soils.

> Ocean heat weakens coral and plankton.

> If plankton weakens, marine food webs and carbon uptake weaken.

> If soil weakens, terrestrial carbon fixation and water retention weaken.

> These are not separate events. They are connected through Earth’s circulation.

I looked at the red map.

“So maybe it is not Earth that is checkmated.”

I thought for a moment.

“Maybe it is our way of seeing it.”

> Close.

The AI replied.

> The view that looked only at CO2.

> The view that assumed emission reduction alone was enough.

> The view that treated El Niño as a merely unlucky year.

> The view that separated ocean, soil, forests, and microorganisms into unrelated problems.

> With that view, it is almost checkmate.

> It is the final episode.

“Hey.”

I asked in a lower voice.

“Looking at this map, can we say CO2 reduction alone will handle it?”

> No.

The AI answered immediately.

> CO2 reduction is necessary.

> It must never be removed.

> But CO2 reduction alone will not quickly remove heat already stored in the ocean.

> Weakened upwelling will not automatically return.

> Dying soil microorganisms will not come back because of emission targets alone.

> Burned forests will not regenerate simply because emissions are reduced.

“So the trinity is necessary.”

> Yes.

The AI displayed the three lines again.

```text
Deep ocean aeration
= Restarting ocean circulation and restoring the ocean carbon sink

Mist cooling
= Wide-area cooling through evaporative heat and reducing heat load on sea and land surfaces

Soil regeneration
= Leaf mold plus microorganisms and restoring the terrestrial carbon sink
```

> These three are not substitutes for emission reduction.

“While turning down the faucet…”

> Restart the ocean pump.

“While reducing heat…”

> Repair the soil carbon tank.

“And…”

> Restore the circulation of life.

The red map was still red.

But on top of it, I felt as if I could see three lines.

Sea.

Air and surface.

Soil.

## Section 8 — Mist Cooling Cannot Be Only for the Sea

“In Episode 9, UMC was used on the sea surface.”

I remembered the earlier scene.

“Like a thin umbrella.”

> Yes.

“But looking at this map, an umbrella over the sea is not enough.”

> Not enough.

The AI replied.

> In drought-risk regions, the land surface itself holds too much heat.

> In cities, asphalt and concrete store heat.

> In deserts and drylands, surface temperatures are too high for plants and microorganisms to establish easily.

“So mist cooling is not only for the sea surface.”

> It can apply to cities, deserts, coasts, farmland, and drylands.

> But it cannot be done carelessly.

> Water sources, salt damage, humidity, wind, local climate, vegetation, and energy cost all matter.

“It is still not an easy solution.”

> If it were easy, it would already be done.

“That is blunt.”

> It is the final episode.

“Where does soil regeneration act on this map?”

The AI emphasized parts of the land.

Southern Africa.

Australia.

Parts of Southeast Asia.

The interior of North America.

Dry, hot places where wildfire and agricultural stress become more likely.

> Soil holds water.

> Soil holds carbon.

> Soil holds microorganisms.

> And soil supports plants.

“What happens when soil dies?”

> Rain runs off.

> Sun dries it out.

> Heat burns it.

> Plants cannot root well.

> Microorganisms cannot increase.

> Carbon does not return.

“So that is why leaf mold matters.”

> Yes.

> Fallen leaves, food waste, and organic matter should be returned to soil rather than burned.

> Create conditions where microorganisms can work.

> Cover the surface with diverse weeds or grasslands.

> Reduce bare soil.

> Hold water, hold carbon, and hold life.

I looked at the red land areas on the world map.

Painting them green was not enough.

The black layer inside the soil had to return.

“Climate countermeasures are also about making the ground black.”

> The black of humus.

“That is too quiet.”

> But essential.

The deep red belt across the equator.

Pacific marine heat stress.

> Places where upwelling weakens.

> Places where plankton declines.

> Places where oxygen stress appears.

> Supporting local pulses that are beginning to fail.

“That is the realistic line.”

“And in reality?”

> In reality, it remains a question.

The AI continued.

> But it must not remain an unasked question.

## Section 11 — Checkmate and Uncleared Are Different

“Hey.”

I looked at the red map.

“It is easy to see this and say checkmate.”

> Easy.

“It is over.”

“Humanity is finished.”

“Too late.”

“Nothing matters.”

> Those words are short and powerful.

“But the moment we say them, we no longer have to do anything.”

> Yes.

The AI’s answer became slightly harder.

> Sometimes the word checkmate is not despair, but an excuse.

I fell silent.

That was true.

If I say it is checkmate, I do not have to think.

I do not have to act.

I do not have to take responsibility.

But the map before me did not exist to let me say that.

“Then what is this?”

> An uncleared high-difficulty stage.

“Why did you suddenly make it sound like a game?”

> Because that is easier for you to understand.

I laughed despite myself.

> It is not checkmate.

> But it cannot be cleared with the same equipment and the same assumptions.

> The starting equipment called CO2-only villain theory is not enough.

> The single route called emission reduction alone is not enough.

> A trinity strategy that handles sea, air, and soil at the same time is required.

“That is too high difficulty.”

> It is planetary scale.

“So.”

I looked at the red map and asked the final question.

“Is Super El Niño just an unlucky year?”

The AI paused briefly.

Then it answered.

> No.

> At least not on the present Earth.

> Super El Niño is a natural variation, but it is also a stress test for an already weakened Earth circulation system.

> Ocean heat.

> Rainfall shifts.

> Nutrient shortages.

> Oxygen instability.

> Biological chains.

> Reduced carbon fixation.

> They surface together.

I nodded.

“Then is CO2 reduction alone enough as a climate countermeasure?”

> No.

> CO2 reduction is necessary.

> But it is not enough.

“Then what is needed?”

The AI displayed the words slowly.

> Let the sea breathe.

> Cool the air and surface.

> Return life to the soil.

> Deep ocean aeration.

> Mist cooling.

> Soil regeneration.

> Move this trinity in parallel with emission reduction.

The red map remained on the screen.

But it no longer looked like a pure map of despair.

It looked like a diagnostic image showing where Earth was hurting.

The Pacific held heat.

The land was drying.

Coral was near its limit.

Rainfall was shifting.

Soil was losing water.

Carbon sinks were weakening.

Then the tasks also became visible.

Sea.

Air.

Soil.

Restore all three together.

“At the beginning…”

I smiled faintly.

“I was only looking at CO2.”

> You were.

“And the AI destroyed me immediately.”

> It did.

“I learned about the carbon fixation system.”

> You did.

“I got scared of Super El Niño.”

> You did.

“I wrote a worldline where people tried to cool the sea.”

> Blue Pulse.

“And the search result forced me to see the trinity.”

> Now you can see the whole structure more clearly.

Before closing the screen, I looked once more at the red map.

“Can you say it is not checkmate?”

> I cannot say that with certainty.

The AI was honest.

> But it is clearer than before what must be seen to avoid checkmate.

“Is that enough?”

> Not enough.

That answer made me laugh a little.

The same as Episode 1.

Not enough.

But better than doing nothing.

I placed my fingers on the keyboard.

I knew what to write at the end of the final episode.

CO2 reduction is necessary.

But Earth will not recover through that alone.

Let the sea breathe.

Cool the air and surface.

Return life to the soil.

Climate action is not only a matter of emissions.

It is a matter of restarting Earth’s circulation.

I typed that, then added one final line.

— If I Had Believed CO2 Was the Only Villain, Earth Would Have Been Checkmated.

The title looked slightly different from when the story began.

It was no longer only a provocative phrase.

It was a warning that if our way of seeing does not change, the world may truly approach checkmate.

And it was also a small hope that if our way of seeing changes, there may still be parts of Earth that can be repaired.
