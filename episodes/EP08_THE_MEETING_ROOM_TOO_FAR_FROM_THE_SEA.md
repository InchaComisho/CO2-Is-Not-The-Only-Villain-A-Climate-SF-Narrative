# Episode 08 — The Meeting Room That Decides Whether to Continue Is Too Far From the Sea

## Preface

In Episode 7, Blue Pulse Phase 1 began.

A small OBS test area showed slight local surface cooling and a possible increase in plankton activity.

At the same time, no one could say the intervention had absolutely no ecological effect.

Episode 8 follows the next question:

Should the experiment expand to Phase 2, or should it stop here?

That decision is not made by the sea.

It is made in meeting rooms far away from it.

---

“So what are they going to do?”

After the Phase 1 interim report, the news moved on almost immediately.

A typhoon track forecast.

Electricity prices.

An election somewhere.

OBS disappeared from the headline list as quickly as it had entered.

> Of course.

The AI appeared in the corner of the screen.

> “The sea surface cooled by 0.2 degrees” and “plankton increased slightly” are not visually dramatic news.

“Yeah, I get it.”

I put down the remote.

“But the decision to continue or stop is happening somewhere right now, isn’t it?”

> It is.

> In a meeting room far from the sea.

---

## Section 1 — Phase 2 Becomes More Realistic

Several days later, the AI displayed the Phase 2 draft.

Blue Pulse Phase 2:
Expanded observation and small-scale distributed experiments across multiple sea areas.

“Oh, so they’re not going straight to ‘cool half the world’s oceans.’”

> If that kind of proposal could pass, this story would be much easier.

The AI continued.

> Phase 2 has three main points.

> First, repeat Phase 1-scale tests in different sea areas.

> Second, add small-scale UMC tests alongside OBS.

> Third, increase observation buoys, satellite monitoring, and shared data systems.

“So instead of scaling up dramatically, they’re increasing samples.”

> Correct.

> Rather than making one experiment ten times larger, they want to run similar tests in different ocean conditions.

“That’s cautious.”

> Cautious enough to be proposed. Not cautious enough to avoid opposition.

---

## Section 2 — The New Issues Are Where and Who Pays

> There is no overwhelming opposition to the technical structure of Phase 2 itself.

The AI opened another slide.

> The main disputes are twofold.

> Where will the experiments take place?

> Who will pay for them?

“There it is.”

I held my forehead.

“Location and money.”

> First, location.

The AI brought up a map.

---

## Section 3 — Not in Our Sea, or Please Use Our Sea

Several candidate areas were marked.

Coral reef zones frequently hit by marine heatwaves.

Coastal waters near continental shelves.

Island regions facing the open ocean.

> Roughly speaking, there are three responses.

The AI listed them.

> First: not in our sea.

> Regions with coral reefs, tourism, or fisheries are strongly concerned about what happens if something goes wrong.

“If the experiment is blamed for damaging tourism or fishery income, that’s a political disaster.”

> Yes.

> Second: please use our sea.

> Some communities already suffering from collapsing fisheries or repeated marine heatwaves are willing to accept some risk.

“Like Tua’s island.”

> Similar.

> Third: do it somewhere else first and let us watch.

“That is the most human answer.”

> It may also be the most common one.

---

## Section 4 — The Ideal of the Common Ocean and the Reality of EEZs

“But people often say the ocean is the common heritage of humankind.”

> As an ideal, yes.

The AI sounded drier than usual.

> In reality, there are territorial waters and Exclusive Economic Zones, or EEZs.

> Most practical deployments of OBS or UMC would occur inside someone’s maritime jurisdiction.

“So ‘for the planet’ still means ‘in someone’s backyard.’”

> Correct.

> And the power relationship between the one offering the technology and the one lending the sea is not always equal.

That sentence changed the map.

The blue areas no longer looked empty.

They looked owned, contested, protected, feared, needed.

---

## Section 5 — The Air in the Meeting Room Is Heavier Than the Sea

A side meeting at the international summit.

Outside the window was a quiet harbor town.

Inside the room, the air was not quiet at all.

> We recognize the potential of OBS and UMC.

A cautious delegate from a major power spoke first.

> However, expanding to multiple sea areas in Phase 2 is premature.

> Environmental assessment frameworks, responsibility rules, and stop conditions must be established first.

“That is reasonable.”

I muttered at the screen.

> On the other side,

The AI switched the feed.

> Countries already facing severe damage are asking a different question.

> Do you intend to spend another ten years discussing assessment frameworks?

> During those ten years, how many people will be displaced by marine heatwaves, storm surges, and failed fisheries?

There it was again.

Both sides were not wrong.

That was what made the room heavy.

---

## Section 6 — An Invitation Arrives

That night, an email arrived from an unfamiliar sender.

Subject:

Invitation to Observe the Blue Pulse Phase 2 Design Workshop

“What?”

The word came out loud.

> Open it.

The AI said it like it already knew.

The message read:

> We have read the narrative series you have been publishing.

> Your way of organizing the causal relationships among OBS, UMC, ocean heat, and carbon fixation made visible something many engineers and researchers have struggled to explain clearly.

> Would you be willing to observe the Phase 2 design workshop and provide an outside perspective?

“What is this?”

> An invitation.

The AI answered too casually.

> The outside author has been pulled slightly into the field.

---

## Section 7 — The Fear of Responsibility

“Wait, wait, wait.”

I stood up from my chair.

“If I say something there, couldn’t it be used as material by the Blue Pulse supporters?”

> Yes.

“Or by cautious opponents?”

> Also yes.

“So either way, some gradient of responsibility attaches to me.”

> Correct.

> That is why remaining only the author of a story and becoming one participant are different things.

“I’m honestly scared.”

> That is a healthy reaction.

The AI paused.

> But there is one premise you should not forget.

“What premise?”

> You cannot completely escape the responsibility of doing nothing either.

“That’s a harsh way to put it.”

> You saw the situation, understood the causal structure, and chose to write about it. That does not make you responsible for the whole project. But it also means you are no longer completely unrelated.

I had no convenient reply.

---

## Section 8 — The Workshop Room Is Just a Room

Several weeks later, I accepted the invitation.

The workshop venue was more ordinary than I expected.

Long tables.

Cheap carpet.

A whiteboard.

A projector.

“I imagined something more like a science fiction facility.”

> Reality often begins in rooms like this.

The AI spoke through my laptop screen.

Aoi sat near the front.

Beside her was an engineer from the UMC side.

Representatives from small island and coastal states were connected online.

I sat quietly in a chair near the back.

My name tag said only:

Observer.

---

## Section 9 — Turning What Was Written as a Story Into a Diagram

During the workshop, Aoi suddenly turned toward me.

> I would like to ask the author of the recent narrative series for something.

“Wait. I have a turn?”

I shouted internally.

Too late.

> Could you draw the causal structure you used in the story on the whiteboard?

A marker was placed in my hand.

When I stood in front of the board, the air in the room seemed to shift.

“Uh… okay.”

I took a breath and began drawing arrows.

“Sea surface heating leads to stronger stratification.”

“Stronger stratification reduces vertical mixing.”

“Reduced mixing can increase marine heatwave risk and weaken nutrient supply.”

“OBS enters here.”

I drew another arrow.

“Air is sent from above, bubbles are released at depth, and bubble plumes provide a localized boost to vertical mixing.”

“UMC enters here.”

I drew a line above the sea surface.

“It may reduce incoming surface heat slightly in targeted areas.”

The board filled with arrows.

From the corner of my laptop, the AI displayed a small message.

> Mostly correct.

“Mostly?”

> Continue.

---

## Section 10 — Outside the Meeting Room, There Is No Smell of the Sea

By the time the workshop ended, the whiteboard was covered with lines and notes.

Support, opposition, caution, hope, distrust, urgency.

All of them had been written over one another.

After leaving the room, I muttered to myself.

“You know…”

“There’s no smell of the sea outside this room at all.”

> No.

The AI’s words appeared on my phone.

> We have been discussing the ocean, but outside the window there are only street trees and buildings.

> Still, decisions made in rooms like this may change the color of distant waters years later.

“Don’t say heavy things casually.”

> You stepping into that room was also one arrow in the causal diagram.

I stopped walking and looked up at the sky.

Somewhere beyond the clouds, there was an ocean.

For the first time, it felt slightly closer.

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Episode 8 showed how the decision to continue or stop Blue Pulse moves away from the open sea and into meeting rooms.

The issues shift from technology alone to location, cost, jurisdiction, responsibility, and trust.

The protagonist also takes a small but important step: from the outside author of a story to an observer inside the process.

The point of this episode is that technological decisions are rarely made only on technical grounds. They are made by people, institutions, communities, and governments — often far from the places most affected.

The next episode moves to one of those places: the Humboldt Current region off Chile, where Phase 2 begins to test artificial upwelling in an El Niño-affected sea.
