# Intervention Ethics

## Short Definition

Intervention ethics in this story means the ethical tensions that arise when humans consider active intervention in Earth systems — particularly the ocean — to respond to climate change. The story does not resolve these tensions. It presents them as real, difficult, and unavoidable.

## Role in the Narrative

Intervention ethics runs through Episodes 6–10 as the central human drama of the Blue Pulse arc. The question is not only whether OBS and UMC could work technically, but whether it is right to try, who has the authority to decide, who bears the consequences, and what it means to do nothing when the alternative is dangerous.

Key moments:

- **Episode 6** — Blue Pulse hits its first wall: governance, public trust, who decides
- **Episode 7** — the first OBS unit starts; the ocean's ambiguous response forces the team to interpret uncertain data
- **Episode 8** — the decision meeting is held far from the sea; people who will never see the water make choices about it
- **Episode 9** — Chile: a new region, new stakeholders, new questions of local consent and sovereignty
- **Episode 10** — the protagonist must decide how far to stay involved; the risk of action is weighed against the risk of doing nothing

## Conceptual Meaning

Inside Master / InchaComisho's framework, intervention ethics involves several distinct tensions:

**Acting vs. not acting**
> "Doing nothing is not automatically neutral."
The story insists that inaction also has consequences. The question is not whether to bear risk, but how to distribute it and who bears it.

**Local consent and sovereignty**
Ocean interventions affect fisheries, coastlines, and communities. Who has the authority to consent? How is local voice incorporated when the intervention is global?

**Ecological uncertainty**
Even with monitoring (OTU), the ocean's response is slow and ambiguous. At what level of certainty is it ethical to act? To stop?

**Responsibility across borders**
Climate effects cross national boundaries. The risk of intervention may fall on people who had no role in the decision.

**Trust**
Public trust in institutions matters. A technically sound intervention that bypasses democratic deliberation can be harmful in ways that the technology itself is not.

**The line**
"Where to draw the line" is the phrase the story keeps returning to. Intervention ethics is not about finding an answer; it is about keeping the question visible.

## Similar or Adjacent Real-World Ideas

Similar or adjacent ideas include:

- geoengineering governance research
- science and technology ethics
- environmental justice
- transboundary environmental governance
- precautionary principle debates in environmental policy
- risk governance in complex systems

These are not identical to the intervention ethics framing in this narrative. The story's framework is specifically built around the emotional and relational dimensions of intervention, not only the institutional or policy dimensions.

## Cautions

- This concept page describes ethical themes in a fictional narrative, not a policy analysis.
- The story does not provide answers to the ethical questions it raises.
- Real-world governance of climate interventions involves international law, scientific review processes, and democratic deliberation that go far beyond what the story depicts.
