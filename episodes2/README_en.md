Part 2 continues from the conclusion of Part 1: CO₂ reduction is necessary, but not sufficient.

Instead of searching for a single perfect climate solution, this part looks back at what civilization has weakened: forests, soil, watersheds, microbial cycles, carbon fixation, and the natural cooling functions of Earth.

The Trinity Model is reinterpreted here not as a miracle technology, but as auxiliary wheels:

```text
Let the sea breathe.
Cool the air and surface.
Return life to the soil.
```

## Part 2 Summary

Part 2 begins by looking directly at climate graphs and asking whether existing climate measures are truly changing the direction of the crisis.

From there, the story widens its view. It follows the historical expansion of population, agriculture, industry, and urban systems, and asks what natural functions were treated as invisible background infrastructure.

Forests cooled the land. Soil held water and carbon. Microorganisms helped maintain fertility and circulation. Rivers connected land to sea. The ocean absorbed heat and carbon, but also carried the burden of what civilization pushed downstream.

By the middle of the part, the story moves from abstract systems to human constraints:

- farmers have harvest schedules, debts, and livelihoods;
- local governments have budgets, disaster seasons, and residents’ demands;
- companies have quarterly numbers, contracts, brands, and supply-chain risk;
- citizens have shopping baskets, price tags, fatigue, habits, and limited attention.

The conclusion is not that everyone can become perfectly sustainable. It is that each actor can bring a small part of the repair:

- farmers can test soil restoration in limited fields;
- municipalities can connect soil to flood control and watershed planning;
- companies can treat soil and water as supply-chain risk;
- citizens can occasionally vote for soil through food choices;
- small communities can create loose purchasing networks.

Part 2 ends by rejecting the search for a single climate solution. Instead, it accepts auxiliary wheels: imperfect, partial, limited, but still better than doing nothing.
