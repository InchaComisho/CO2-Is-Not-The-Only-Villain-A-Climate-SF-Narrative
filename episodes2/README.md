Instead of immediately expanding the Trinity Model as a future technology, Part 2 looks backward into the history of civilization.

It asks why such auxiliary measures became necessary in the first place:

```text
What did civilization increase?
What did civilization reduce?
What natural functions were treated as background infrastructure?
What can still be repaired?
```

## Part 2 Concept

Part 1 reached the Trinity Model:

```text
Let the sea breathe.
Cool the air and surface.
Return life to the soil.
```

Part 2 examines the historical background behind that need:

- CO₂ emissions and the limits of reduction-only framing
- population growth after the Industrial Revolution
- deforestation and land-use conversion
- loss of forest cooling, soil retention, and carbon fixation functions
- chemical fertilizer, pesticide, and monoculture dependency
- nutrient runoff into rivers and oceans
- red tides, hypoxic water masses, and dead zones
- civilization as a system that externalized natural functions
