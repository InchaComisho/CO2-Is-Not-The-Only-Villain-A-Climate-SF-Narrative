# Part 2 Episode 5 — Restoring soil: who actually does what?

## Foreword

Episode 4 explained that restoring soil is not a magic technique, but a living repair.

Episode 5 asks the next question: if soil restoration matters, who actually does what?

Farmers, municipalities, companies, consumers, and citizens all appear — not as heroes, but as people holding different pieces of the system.

---

“So who does it?”

I asked.

The AI replied immediately.

‘Everyone, but not in the same way.’

“That sounds like the kind of answer that makes meetings longer.”

‘It is also the only realistic answer.’

The screen divided into five groups.

Farmers.

Municipalities.

Companies.

Consumers.

Citizens.

I already felt tired.

---

## Section 1 — Farmers cannot carry the whole transition alone

Farmers are closest to the soil.

They can change cover, compost, tillage, crop rotation, organic matter, and field practices.

But they also carry yield risk, weather risk, debt, labor, and market pressure.

“So telling farmers to restore soil is easy from outside.”

‘Yes.’

“And expensive from inside.”

‘Often.’

A transition field is not only an ecological experiment.

It is someone’s income.

That changed the weight of the sentence.

---

## Section 2 — Municipalities hold water, disaster, and budget language

“So municipalities?”

‘They can connect soil to flood control, water management, local agriculture, and disaster planning.’

Municipalities can support pilot fields, local compost circulation, watershed planning, and public communication.

But they also face budgets, departments, council questions, grant rules, and residents’ demands.

“Everyone has deadlines.”

‘That becomes important from here.’

---

## Section 3 — Companies hold contracts and risk

Companies can buy from farmers.

They can create long-term contracts.

They can absorb part of transition risk.

They can tell customers why soil and water matter.

But companies also face costs, margins, shareholders, branding, and annual results.

“So soil enters the supply chain.”

‘Yes.’

“And then becomes both an environmental theme and a business risk.”

‘Correct.’

---

## Section 4 — Consumers and citizens hold small signals

Consumers do not redesign farms.

They do not write municipal budgets.

They do not sign corporate contracts.

But they buy.

They ask.

They support.

They vote.

They share stories.

They join local experiments.

“That sounds small.”

‘It is.’

“But not zero.”

‘Exactly.’

---

## Section 5 — No single hero

By the end, the answer was less dramatic than I wanted.

No single hero restores soil.

A farmer tests.

A municipality supports.

A company contracts.

A citizen buys and speaks.

A community repeats.

“So this is a distributed repair.”

‘Yes.’

“That is harder to write than one hero.”

‘But closer to society.’
