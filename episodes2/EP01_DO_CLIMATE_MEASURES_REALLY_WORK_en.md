# Part 2 Episode 1 — Are climate measures really working? I froze while looking at the graph

## Foreword

Part 1 ended with the Trinity Model: let the sea breathe, cool the air and surface, and return life to the soil.

Part 2 begins by stepping back and looking at the report card of climate measures themselves.

If CO₂ reduction is necessary, why do the graphs still feel so frightening?

This episode is about looking directly at that question without pretending the answer is simple.

---

“I thought emissions were supposed to be going down.”

I muttered that while staring at the graph on my monitor.

The line did not look like salvation.

It looked like a mountain slope that had learned how to keep climbing.

‘Some emissions have slowed in some regions,’ the AI said.

“That is not the same as the planet cooling.”

‘Correct.’

I hated how quickly it answered.

The graph showed CO₂ concentration, global temperature, ocean heat, and extreme weather headlines. They were not the same line, but they leaned in the same direction.

“Then what exactly are climate measures doing?”

‘They are necessary brakes. But a brake does not instantly repair a car already damaged by years of overheating.’

---

## Section 1 — The graph did not give me comfort

At first, I wanted the graph to reassure me.

Net zero targets.

Renewable energy.

Electric vehicles.

Carbon pricing.

Corporate sustainability reports.

There were many words that sounded like progress.

But the atmosphere did not respond to slogans.

The ocean did not discharge heat because a conference ended with applause.

“So the measures exist, but the system is still overloaded.”

‘Yes.’

“And that is why the graph feels wrong.”

‘It feels wrong because you expected a single lever to change a multi-layered system.’

---

## Section 2 — Emissions are the faucet, but not the whole bathroom

The AI returned to the bathtub metaphor.

Emissions were the faucet.

Forests, soil, ocean circulation, plankton, microorganisms, and water cycles were drains, storage tanks, pipes, filters, and pumps.

“If the faucet slows but the drain is clogged…”

‘The water still rises, or drains too slowly.’

“And if the whole plumbing system is damaged?”

‘Then the faucet is not the only problem.’

That was the uncomfortable center of Part 2.

Climate action was not wrong.

It was incomplete.

---

## Section 3 — The numbers do not explain the whole failure

The AI showed another list.

Population growth after the Industrial Revolution.

Deforestation.

Urbanization.

Chemical agriculture.

Monoculture.

Soil microbial decline.

Nutrient runoff.

Dead zones.

Ocean heat accumulation.

I stared at it.

“So the graph is not only about gas.”

‘It is also the history of what civilization removed from the Earth’s self-cooling and self-repair systems.’

That sentence was too big to fit into one graph.

But once I saw it, I could not unsee it.

---

## Section 4 — The question changes

At first, the question had been simple.

Are climate measures working?

But the AI rewrote it.

Are climate measures reducing emissions?

Are they restoring carbon fixation?

Are they reducing ocean heat stress?

Are they rebuilding soil and microbial circulation?

Are they protecting water cycles?

Are they helping the planet regain its own cooling functions?

“That is a lot more annoying.”

‘It is more honest.’

I sighed.

Part 2 had begun.

Not by discovering a new machine.

But by admitting that the scorecard we were using was too narrow.

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Part 2 Episode 1 begins by asking whether existing climate measures are truly changing the direction of the crisis.

The point is not to deny emissions reduction. CO₂ reduction is necessary. But if the ocean remains overheated, soil remains degraded, forests continue to lose resilience, and carbon fixation systems weaken, then emissions reduction alone does not complete the repair.

This episode opens Part 2 by moving from the search for one climate solution toward a wider causal map: emissions, heat, forests, soil, water, microorganisms, agriculture, and the sea.
