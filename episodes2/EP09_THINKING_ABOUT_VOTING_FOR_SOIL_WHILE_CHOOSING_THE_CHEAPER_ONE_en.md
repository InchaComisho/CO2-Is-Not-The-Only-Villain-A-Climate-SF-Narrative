# Part 2 Episode 9 — While choosing the cheaper one, I thought about the day I would “vote for soil”

## Foreword

Episodes 6, 7, and 8 looked at the deadlines of farmers, municipalities, and companies.

Episode 9 brings the story down to the citizen’s daily life: the supermarket shelf.

Price tags, discount stickers, points, habits, fatigue, and a vague desire to choose something better all meet in the shopping basket.

---

“This is impossible.”

I muttered in front of the vegetable section.

Tomatoes.

Cucumbers.

Lettuce.

Spinach.

Price tags.

Origin labels.

Discount stickers.

Somewhere in all of this, I was supposed to think about soil.

‘At last, you have reached the shopping basket,’ the AI said.

“I did not come here for a moral trial.”

‘Yet here you are.’

---

## Section 1 — There are many labels, but soil is invisible

Organic.

Reduced pesticide.

Local.

Contract farmer.

Seasonal.

Good value.

The labels were everywhere.

But none of them said:

“This vegetable comes from soil with improved structure and reduced watershed load.”

“That would be too long,” I said.

‘And hard to understand in five seconds.’

A slightly more expensive package said “environmentally considerate cultivation.”

“What does that actually mean?”

‘That is the wall between intention and action.’

---

## Section 2 — Is choosing the cheaper one wrong?

“I have to be honest.”

‘Go ahead.’

“I do not think choosing the cheaper one is evil.”

‘Of course not.’

It was daily shopping.

Electricity bills existed.

Month-end existed.

Fatigue existed.

Habit existed.

If every purchase had to become a perfect ethical decision, I would probably give up in a few days.

‘That is decision cost.’

“Exactly.”

The AI did not blame me.

That made the problem feel more real.

---

## Section 3 — The deadlines meet at the shelf

The farmer’s deadline.

The municipality’s budget.

The company’s accounts.

All of them seemed to gather at the price tag.

“This shelf is more crowded than it looks.”

‘Good observation.’

The price contained labor, logistics, contracts, weather, risk, and sometimes pressure pushed onto someone else.

I could not see the soil.

But that did not mean it was absent.

---

## Section 4 — The shopping basket is not exactly a ballot box, but it is not unrelated either

‘Purchasing behavior sends signals.’

“I hate when you make shopping sound heavy.”

‘It is not a perfect vote.’

“But not unrelated.”

‘Correct.’

If soil-friendly products never sell, companies will not expand them.

If no one asks about soil, labels will not improve.

If no one supports farmers trying to change, the risk remains on them.

A shopping basket is small.

But it is not zero.

---

## Section 5 — Once a month may be enough to begin

At the register, I looked at my basket.

It was ordinary.

Discount eggs.

Cheap tofu.

Normal milk.

Vegetables chosen mostly by habit and price.

“This is not exactly voting for soil.”

‘No.’

“But maybe once a month.”

‘Once a month?’

“Once a month, I look more carefully. Maybe buy from a local farmer, a direct market, a visible soil project, or something whose story I can understand.”

‘That is more sustainable than perfectionism.’

I wrote it down.

Once a month, vote for soil.

Small.

But possible.

---

## Afterword

Thank you for reading.

Episode 9 brings the soil and watershed story into everyday shopping. It does not blame people for choosing cheaper options. Price, habit, fatigue, information load, and household budgets are real.

The episode proposes a modest practice: not perfect consumption, but an occasional, repeated “vote for soil.”
